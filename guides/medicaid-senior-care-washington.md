If you're trying to figure out how your parent can afford care in Washington once savings run low, here's the short answer: Washington's Medicaid program pays for nursing [home care](/guides/in-home-care-explained/), and — more than in many states — it also pays for care in [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/) and [adult family homes](/guides/adult-family-homes/). This page explains how, and how to get started.

## What Washington's Medicaid program is called

Washington's Medicaid program is **Apple Health**, overseen by the Health Care Authority. But for long-term care, the agency you'll actually deal with is the **Department of Social and Health Services (DSHS)** — specifically its **Aging and Long-Term Support Administration (ALTSA)** and local **Home and Community Services (HCS)** offices. They handle both the financial application and the care assessment for long-term services and supports.

Having regular Apple Health coverage does not automatically qualify someone for long-term care help — that requires a separate application and assessment.

## Yes, it pays for nursing homes

For eligible residents, Apple Health pays for care in Medicaid-certified [nursing homes](/guides/skilled-nursing/), including room and board. Most of your parent's monthly income then goes toward the cost of care ("participation"), with a small personal needs allowance retained. This coverage is an entitlement — if your parent qualifies, it pays, with no waitlist.

Remember that Medicare only covers short rehab stays after a hospitalization; it is not long-term care coverage. Our [Medicaid vs. Medicare guide](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/) explains the difference.

## Assisted living and adult family homes: COPES

Washington is one of the stronger states for Medicaid coverage outside nursing homes. Its main program is a waiver called **COPES** (Community Options Program Entry System), which pays for care services for people who qualify for nursing-home-level care but live in:

- Their own home or a family member's home
- **Adult family homes** — small licensed homes (typically up to 6–8 residents), a major part of Washington's care landscape
- **Assisted living facilities** and enhanced adult residential care settings

Washington also uses the federal **Community First Choice** option and **Medicaid Personal Care** to fund in-home personal care, and has other specialized waivers. Your HCS case manager will sort out which program fits — you don't need to pick the program yourself; you need to ask for a long-term care assessment.

The universal caveat: in assisted living and adult family homes, Medicaid pays for **care services**, not the full **room and board**. Room and board comes from your parent's own income at a state-capped rate — far below private-pay prices. And not every facility takes Medicaid: some don't participate at all, and others require a year or more of private pay first. Ask directly, early, and get the answer in writing.

> **Ask this:** "Do you accept COPES/Medicaid residents from day one — and if my mom private-pays now and runs out of money in two years, can she stay?"

## Who qualifies: the basic shape

Two doors, both must open:

- **Care need:** a DSHS assessor evaluates whether your parent needs nursing-facility-level care — substantial help with daily activities or supervision for cognitive decline.
- **Finances:** income and asset limits apply. A single applicant's countable assets generally must be very low (around $2,000 in recent years), with income limits tied to federal figures that change annually. A spouse remaining at home keeps a much larger protected share of assets and income under spousal impoverishment rules.

Washington applies the standard **five-year look-back**: gifts and below-market transfers within five years of applying can trigger a penalty period. Don't transfer assets before getting professional advice. Exact dollar figures change every year — confirm current limits with DSHS or an elder law attorney.

## Waitlists and capacity

COPES enrollment is technically capped, but Washington has historically funded it well enough that eligible people are generally served — the state has long pushed home and community care over institutions. The practical constraint is finding a facility or adult family home with an open Medicaid bed at the Medicaid rate, particularly in high-cost areas like Seattle and the Eastside. Confirm current program capacity with your HCS office, and expect the facility search to take longer than the paperwork.

## How to apply

- Contact your local **DSHS Home and Community Services (HCS) office** — this is the front door for long-term care Medicaid. You can also apply online via Washington Connection.
- The process has two tracks that can run in parallel: the **financial application** (Apple Health long-term care application) and the **care assessment** (request it as soon as the financial application is submitted — don't wait for approval).
- Your local **Area Agency on Aging** can help you understand options, and often manages ongoing case management for in-home clients.

Gather several years of bank statements, income records, property documents, and insurance policies before starting. Start the process before the money is gone — the review takes weeks even when everything goes smoothly.

## Estate recovery

After a Medicaid recipient dies, Washington seeks repayment from their estate for long-term care costs it paid — which can reach the family home. There are protections while a spouse is alive and other exceptions, but this is a real consideration. Read our [Medicaid estate recovery guide](/guides/medicaid-estate-recovery/) and raise it with an elder law attorney before making decisions about the house.

## Common questions

**Does Washington Medicaid pay for assisted living?**
Yes — through COPES, for people who meet care and financial rules, in facilities that accept Medicaid. Your parent's income still covers room and board at a state-set rate, and participating facilities can be limited.

**What's an adult family home, and is it covered?**
A licensed home caring for a small number of residents in a residential house — Washington has thousands of them. COPES pays for care in adult family homes, and for many families they're the most realistic Medicaid-funded option outside a nursing home.

**Can my mom keep her house and still qualify?**
Often yes while she or her spouse lives there — the home is generally not a countable asset in that situation — but estate recovery may apply later. Get elder law advice before selling, transferring, or borrowing against it.

**How long does this take?**
Expect several weeks to a few months from application to services, depending on paperwork completeness and assessment scheduling. Incomplete financial documentation is the most common delay.

## Where to get help

- **DSHS Home and Community Services (HCS)** — applications and care assessments (find your regional office at dshs.wa.gov)
- **Area Agencies on Aging** — local options counseling and case management
- **SHIBA** (Washington's SHIP program) — free Medicare and insurance counseling
- **Eldercare Locator:** 1-800-677-1116
- An **elder law attorney** — for spend-down, spousal protection, and estate recovery planning
