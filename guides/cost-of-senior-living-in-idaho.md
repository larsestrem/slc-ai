Here's some rare good news in senior care: Idaho is one of the most affordable states in the West, with [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/) running thousands of dollars a month below Oregon and Washington. If your family is spread across the Northwest, Idaho's prices are worth a serious look before you sign anywhere.

## What senior care costs in Idaho

Statewide medians from the 2024 CareScout (Genworth) Cost of Care Survey - half of providers charge more, half less, and Boise prices differently than small-town Idaho:

- **Assisted living:** about $4,600/month ($55,200/year) - far below the national median of roughly $5,900, and $2,400-$2,700/month below Oregon and Washington
- **[In-home care](/guides/in-home-care-explained/):** Idaho hourly rates have recently surveyed around $32/hour for homemaker services and $34/hour for a home health aide; at the survey's standard 44 hours/week that works out to roughly **$6,100-$6,500/month** (our calculation from hourly figures - treat as an estimate)
- **[Memory care](/guides/memory-care/):** no official state median; using the typical assisted-living-plus-20-30% premium, plan on roughly **$5,500-$6,000/month** (our estimate, not a survey figure)
- **Nursing home, semi-private room:** about $10,068/month ($120,815/year)
- **Nursing home, private room:** about $10,707/month ($128,480/year)

Notice the pattern: Idaho's assisted living is a genuine bargain, but its nursing homes cost close to the national rate ($9,300-$10,600/month in 2024). The savings live in residential care, not [skilled nursing](/guides/skilled-nursing/). Costs nationally kept rising in 2025 (assisted living up 5% to $6,200/month), so expect quotes somewhat above these medians.

## Boise vs. the rest of Idaho

Unusually, the Boise metro is not the expensive part of the state - surveys have repeatedly found Boise and Idaho Falls among Idaho's cheaper markets, thanks to a deep supply of newer communities built during the region's growth boom. Coeur d'Alene and the northern panhandle tend to price higher, pulled up by Spokane-area wages and in-migration. Small-town facilities in eastern and central Idaho vary widely - some are inexpensive, but options thin out fast. Treat city-level figures as estimates and get local quotes.

The insider truth: Idaho is the arbitrage destination for the Northwest. Families in Portland, Seattle, and Spokane routinely move a parent to Boise, Twin Falls, or Coeur d'Alene and save $25,000-$30,000 a year on assisted living. If a sibling already lives in Idaho, that's often the strongest financial move on the table.

> **Ask this:** "What's the all-in monthly price at my parent's assessed care level - and exactly which services trigger a move to the next pricing tier?"

## Will Idaho Medicaid help?

Idaho Medicaid's **Aged & Disabled (A&D) Waiver** is the main program for care outside a nursing home. For people who meet nursing-facility level of care and financial limits (in 2026, roughly $2,982/month income and $2,000 in countable assets for a single applicant), it pays for care services in your home, a **certified family home** (Idaho's adult foster care), or a **residential assisted living facility**. The waiver covers care only - room and board stays on you.

Honest caveats: not all communities accept the waiver, and Medicaid reimbursement rates mean waiver beds cluster in modestly priced buildings. Nursing home care is covered as a standard Medicaid benefit for those who qualify. Eligibility numbers change yearly - verify current rules with Idaho Health and Welfare or an elder law attorney before making plans.

## How to stretch your budget in Idaho

- **Certified family homes are the deepest value.** Idaho certifies small homes (typically 1-4 residents) where care often costs $3,000-$4,000/month all-in - with one caregiver for a handful of residents. For a parent who dislikes big buildings, they're worth touring. Compare options in our [Idaho directory](/directory/idaho/).
- **In Boise, competition is your friend.** With many communities built since 2015, ask two or three for their best rate and watch community fees disappear.
- **Watch the home-care crossover point.** With assisted living at $4,600/month, in-home care crosses over at only about 30 hours a week here - earlier than in pricier states. Run both numbers; see [paying for senior care](/guides/paying-for-senior-care/).
- **Panhandle families: compare against Spokane.** Sometimes the Idaho side of the border is cheaper for equivalent care; sometimes a Spokane Valley community wins. Twenty minutes of driving can be worth hundreds a month.

> **Ask this:** "Do you accept the Aged & Disabled Waiver - immediately, or only after private-pay years - and may I speak with a family whose parent converted to Medicaid here?"

## Common questions

**Why is Idaho assisted living so much cheaper than Oregon's or Washington's?**
Lower caregiver wages, lower real estate costs, and lighter regulatory overhead. The care model is the same; the labor market isn't.

**Is cheap assisted living lower quality?**
Not automatically - price mostly tracks local wages and building costs. Judge each community on staffing, inspection history, and reviews, not its rate.

**How firm is the memory care estimate?**
It's an estimate: no official Idaho memory care median exists, so we applied the typical 20-30% premium over assisted living. Boise-area memory care commonly quotes $5,500-$7,000/month; always price the actual unit.

**Does Medicare pay for assisted living in Idaho?**
No - Medicare never covers assisted living or ongoing custodial care, in Idaho or anywhere. See [Medicaid vs. Medicare](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/).

## Where to get help

The Idaho Commission on Aging and its Area Agencies on Aging offer free options counseling, and the Eldercare Locator is 1-800-677-1116. Idaho SHIBA counselors answer Medicare questions at no cost. For Medicaid eligibility strategy, consult an elder law attorney - rules change, and this page isn't legal or financial advice.
