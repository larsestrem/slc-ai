Most families are surprised to learn that Medicaid, not Medicare, is the largest payer for long-term care in the United States. It covers a majority of nursing home residents and funds much of the home and community-based care that lets older adults stay out of institutions. That is why the Medicaid changes in the 2025 federal budget law, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, are drawing close attention from families, providers, and analysts as they begin to take effect in 2026.

The projected effects are contested and still unfolding, but several analyses point the same direction. A report from the [U.S. Senate Special Committee on Aging](https://www.aging.senate.gov/download/the-devastating-consequences-of-medicaid-cuts-on-older-adults?download=1) warns of consequences for older adults who rely on the program. An industry survey from the [American Health Care Association and NCAL](https://www.ahcancal.org/News-and-Communications/Press-Releases/Pages/New-Survey-Highlights-Devastating-Impact-of-Medicaid-Reductions-on-Nursing-Homes.aspx) found that many nursing home operators expect to reduce the number of Medicaid residents they can serve, cut staffing, or in some cases close, if funding tightens. Researchers at [The New School](https://www.economicpolicyresearch.org/resource-library/research/medicaid-cuts-shift-long-term-care-costs-to-families) argue the changes would shift more long-term care costs onto families, and the [Population Reference Bureau](https://www.prb.org/resource/medicaid-cuts-could-push-vulnerable-older-adults-into-nursing-homes/) notes that reducing home-care funding can push vulnerable older adults into nursing homes when in-home support falls away.

These are projections, not certainties, and how the changes land will depend heavily on how each state administers its Medicaid program. But the through-line matters for planning: if Medicaid-funded home care becomes harder to get, more of the day-to-day cost and caregiving can fall on families.

## What this means for your family

This is a moment to understand where you stand, not to panic. A few steps make sense regardless of how the details shake out.

First, learn how Medicaid actually fits into paying for care. Many families assume Medicare covers long-term care and discover too late that it largely does not. Our guides to [Medicaid versus Medicare](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/) and [how families actually pay for care](/guides/paying-for-senior-care/) explain who pays for what, and [government assistance programs](/guides/government-assistance-programs/) walks through the help that exists beyond Medicaid.

Second, if a family member relies on Medicaid-funded home care, ask your state Medicaid office or [Area Agency on Aging](/guides/government-assistance-programs/) directly about their current benefits and any changes, rather than relying on headlines. Rules, waivers, and waitlists vary a great deal by state, and your caseworker is the authority on your situation.

Third, plan earlier than feels necessary. Long-term care is expensive whether or not Medicaid rules change, and families who understand the [real costs](/guides/cost-of-senior-living/) and their options, including [in-home care](/guides/in-home-care-explained/) and [skilled nursing](/guides/skilled-nursing/), make calmer, better decisions than those forced to choose in a crisis.

We will keep watching how these changes are implemented. The policy debate will continue, but the practical task for families is steady: know how care is paid for, know your state's rules, and plan ahead so you are choosing from a position of information rather than fear.
