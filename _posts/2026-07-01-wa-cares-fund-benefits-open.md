Washington has become the first state in the country to open benefits under a public long-term care insurance program. As the [Office of Governor Bob Ferguson](https://governor.wa.gov/news/2026/wa-cares-fund-benefits-open-launching-nations-first-public-long-term-care-insurance-program) announced, WA Cares Fund benefits became available beginning in July 2026, giving eligible workers a way to help pay for care that most private plans and Medicare do not cover.

According to the [WA Cares Fund](https://wacaresfund.wa.gov/benefits), the full benefit in 2026 is $36,500, and it is set to grow automatically with inflation each year. The program is funded by a payroll contribution of 0.58 percent, and the money can be used for a wide range of long-term care needs: in-home care, home safety modifications, home-delivered meals, and equipment like a walker or a bath chair, as well as services in an [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/) or [adult family home](/guides/adult-family-homes/) setting and around-the-clock care for someone living with [dementia](/guides/memory-care/).

## Who qualifies

Eligibility has two parts: a contribution requirement and a care-needs requirement. On the contribution side, the fund reports that workers who have paid in for at least three years have earned access to the full $36,500. There is also a pathway for people closer to retirement: those born before 1968 can earn partial benefits of $3,650 for each year they contributed. On the care side, you must need help with three or more [activities of daily living](/guides/levels-of-care-explained/), such as bathing, dressing, eating, or managing medications, and be expected to need that help for at least 90 days.

## How to apply

Per the [WA Cares Fund's application guidance](https://wacaresfund.wa.gov/apply), you start by creating an online account, then apply online or over the phone, and you can get in-person help at your local Area Agency on Aging (an appointment may be required). If you have trouble applying online, the fund's help line is 844-CARE4WA, and language assistance is available.

## What this means for your family

If your family is in Washington, this is worth understanding now, before a crisis. Long-term care is expensive, and most people are surprised to learn that Medicare generally does not pay for ongoing help with daily living. WA Cares is not a full solution to that gap, $36,500 does not cover years of care, but it is real money that can pay for in-home help, a home modification that lets someone stay put longer, or part of the cost of assisted living.

A few practical steps. First, check whether the person needing care meets the contribution history and the three-ADL care threshold; the [qualifying page](https://wacaresfund.wa.gov/benefits) walks through both. Second, think about how the benefit fits with other funding, since for many families it will be one piece alongside savings, long-term care insurance, or [Medicaid](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/). Our guide to [how families actually pay for care](/guides/paying-for-senior-care/) lays out the full picture. And because WA Cares can pay for care at home, it is worth reading up on [in-home care](/guides/in-home-care-explained/) as an option, not just residential care.

Wherever you live, this program is a reminder to plan for long-term care before you need it. Washington is first, but several states are studying similar programs, and the underlying problem, that care costs more than most families expect and few plans cover it, is the same everywhere.
