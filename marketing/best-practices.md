This page is the advice we'd give a community director over coffee, whether or not they ever hire us. We run senior.living.community, a senior living directory, and we've spent years watching which marketing actually fills senior living communities and which just spends money. Use freely.

## Which channels actually work, and why

**Search beats social, because intent beats attention.** Families choosing senior care don't discover a community by scrolling; they decide a decision is due, then they search: "assisted living [city]," "memory care near me," "[community name] reviews." Search - organic and paid - puts you in front of people at exactly that moment. Social media has a real but narrow role: showing life inside your community (activities, staff, events) to families already considering you, and reassuring the adult children who will check your Facebook page before touring. Post there for warmth, not for demand.

**Your Google Business Profile is your real homepage.** For most communities, more families see the Google panel - photos, hours, reviews, map - than ever reach the website. Basic hygiene pays disproportionately:

- Claim and verify the profile; make sure name, address, phone match your website exactly
- Choose the right categories (assisted living facility, memory care, etc.)
- Add current, honest photos - rooms, common areas, dining, real activities - and refresh them a few times a year
- Answer the Q&A section yourself before a stranger does
- Keep holiday hours and contact details current

An hour a month here outperforms most paid campaigns per dollar, because it costs nothing.

**Reviews are the #1 local ranking and trust factor.** Google ranks local results heavily on review quantity, recency, and rating - and families weigh them even harder than Google does. A community at 4.6 stars with 80 recent reviews will out-draw a 3.9 with 12 old ones in the same zip code almost regardless of what either spends on advertising. Which brings us to the topic that deserves its own section.

## Reputation, systematically

**Earn positive reviews ethically.** Never buy reviews, never gate ("only ask happy people via a filter tool" violates Google's rules), never pressure. What works and is entirely legitimate: *ask, at the right moment*. When a family member thanks a staff member, when a resident's daughter mentions how well the move went, when a care conference ends warmly - that's the moment to say "It would mean a lot to our team if you'd share that in a Google review," ideally with a direct link sent by email. Make asking a routine, not a campaign. A steady **2-4** new reviews a month compounds into a moat.

**Respond to negative reviews like the whole city is reading - because it is.** Future families judge you less on the complaint than on your reply. Respond promptly, thank the reviewer, acknowledge the frustration without arguing the facts publicly, never disclose health details (that's a privacy obligation, not just tact), and move specifics offline: "Please call our director directly at…" A calm, humane response to a harsh review is some of the best marketing copy you'll ever publish.

**The root of good reviews is right-fit admissions.** Most negative reviews trace back to mismatched expectations: a resident whose care needs exceeded what the community could provide, a family who expected a different culture or level of attention. You cannot review-manage your way out of admitting people your community isn't right for. When admissions takes the time to confirm the fit - level of care, culture, family expectations - residents are happier, families are calmer, and reviews take care of themselves. This is also simply the right way to treat families making one of the hardest decisions of their lives.

## The referral-fee math vs. owning your inquiry flow

Referral platforms charge communities roughly **$3,000-6,000+** per move-in - about a month's rent. They deliver, and if your census is hurting they're hard to refuse. But run the comparison honestly: in this niche, search ads land visitors actively looking for care in your city for **$1-3** per click. Even with conservative conversion assumptions, a self-generated move-in typically costs a fraction of one referral fee - and every inquiry that comes to *you* directly is one where you aren't competing inside a referral advisor's inbox with three other communities.

The strategic point matters more than any single fee: your inquiry flow is an asset. Rent it from a platform and it evaporates the day you stop paying. Build it - through search, your Google profile, and reviews - and it appreciates. Use referral platforms as overflow if you must; don't let them be the foundation. (Full channel-by-channel numbers in our [cost comparison](/marketing/cost-comparison/).)

## Train your own team vs. paying per placement

The same rent-vs-own logic applies to sales. External advisors paid per placement will always optimize for *a* placement; your own team can optimize for the *right* one. Realistic guidance for building in-house capability:

- **Answer the phone like it's the whole job**, because for that family it is. A missed call at 4:50 pm on Friday is a tour your competitor gives Saturday morning. Track speed-to-answer and speed-to-callback; they predict outcomes better than any script.
- **No overpromising, ever.** Don't gloss over pricing, care limitations, or waitlists to get the tour. Families find out, and the cost arrives later as a move-out and a one-star review. Honest answers lose a few tours and win the right ones.
- **Follow-up discipline beats charisma.** Most inquiries aren't ready for **30-90** days. A simple cadence - same-day thank you, a check-in a week later, a genuinely useful note monthly (not "just following up," but "here's our guide to comparing memory care") - wins families that a one-call-and-done competitor forgets.
- **Tours should show life, not architecture.** Introduce a staff member by name, walk through during an activity, let the community's actual warmth do the selling.

None of this requires a hired gun. It requires that someone owns it, measures it, and treats every family with the respect the moment deserves.

## The quality flywheel

Everything above is downstream of one conviction: **maximizing care quality is the growth strategy.** The sequence is mechanical once you see it:

Great care → happy residents and families → honest positive reviews → higher search visibility and trust → more direct inquiries → demand exceeds capacity → admissions can focus purely on fit → better-matched residents → even better outcomes and care.

A waitlist isn't vanity. An empty bed at **$6,000**/month bleeds roughly **$200 a day**, and a full census removes the quiet desperation that pushes communities into poor-fit admissions and eventually into the reviews that undo years of work. Marketing - good search presence, a tended Google profile, a systematic review habit - doesn't create this flywheel. It accelerates one that only spins if the care is genuinely good. If the care isn't there yet, fix that first; no channel can outrun it, and no channel should.

## Staffing: the marketing problem nobody calls marketing

Every operator says hiring and retention is their hardest problem - and most treat it as
pure HR. Half of it is. Retention comes from inside the building: fair schedules posted
early, leadership that works the floor on hard days, recognizing the aides residents love,
real growth paths from caregiver to med tech to lead. Happier staff make happier residents;
happier residents make the job worth keeping. That loop is yours to build, not ours.

But the other half is genuinely marketing: most communities' "careers" presence is a PDF
and an Indeed post. The same targeted-search machinery that brings families brings
applicants - campaigns aimed at caregivers within commuting distance, a careers page that
loads fast and applies in two minutes, and job content that says what shifts actually pay.
Communities that treat applicant flow like admissions flow stop hiring from desperation - which is exactly how they stop losing their best people to burnout from running short.

## Resident life: programs are a magnet, not a cost

Engagement isn't a nice-to-have - residents who stay mentally and physically active
genuinely do better, and families choose (and review) communities based on what daily life
looks like. You don't have to build it all in-house: local yoga and chair-fitness
instructors, salons, music and dance programs, gardening clubs, and intergenerational
visits can be brought in. If finding those providers is the bottleneck, that's a targeted
outreach problem - the same discipline as finding residents, pointed at a different
audience - and a real activity calendar becomes some of the best marketing content a
community can publish: it photographs well, it answers the question every family silently
asks on tour, and it's true.
