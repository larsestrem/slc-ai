Questions to Ask on a Tour

The tour is the community's sales presentation. These are the questions that cut past it - the ones that reveal what daily life would actually be like for your own parent. Tick them off as you go, or and bring it to every visit.

Keep it about your parent. You are not there to audit every resident or inspect the whole operation - the state does that, and you can read the results on the community's official record before you go. Your job on the tour is narrower and more useful: picture one specific person, your parent, living here, and ask how their day, their care, and their concerns would be handled. Do that paper homework first so the visit confirms or contradicts the record rather than replacing it.

After the visit, before you drive off: sit in the car and write your honest impression in each section's notes box below, while it's fresh. After three tours the buildings blur together, and in a stressful search you won't remember which place had the quiet dining room or the caregiver who knew everyone's name. Writing it down now is what lets you compare fairly later, instead of straining to recall which facility was which.

Save it, but print it. Your entries save in this browser so they're here when you come back - but that copy lives only on this device, holds one community at a time, and can be lost if you clear your browser, switch devices, or start notes for the next tour. Print or save a PDF of each community's notes at your earliest opportunity, then clear and start fresh for the next visit.

The person's daily life

Start here. A community that gets daily life right for your parent is worth more than a beautiful lobby.

Care & staffing

People provide care, not granite countertops. Push for numbers, by shift.

Ask a front-line aide, not the tour guide: "How long have you worked here?" If nobody is past their first year, you have the turnover number without asking management. Ask residents and visiting families: "When you press the button, how long does it take?"

When needs change

Care needs almost always grow. Moving twice is hard on everyone.

How they handle concerns

Every community gets complaints. What matters is what happens next. Process beats promises. (why good communities get complaints)

The real cost & the contract

Get every number in writing, at your parent's assessed care level - never the advertised starting rate. (what's not included)

Before you decide

How to use this list well

You will not get to every question on one visit, and that is fine - the point is to steer the conversation toward the things that matter to the person who will live there, not the chandelier. Watch how a community answers as much as what it answers: a straight number, even an imperfect one, tells you more than a warm non-answer. When you are ready to compare finalists side by side, the comparison worksheet turns these answers into a fair scorecard, and the touring checklist covers what to watch for with your own eyes while you ask.
