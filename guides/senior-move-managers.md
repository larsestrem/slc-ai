Somewhere between "we chose a community" and "Mom is settled in her new room" sits a mountain: a house full of fifty years of belongings that has to become one small room's worth. Most families don't know there is an entire profession built for exactly this. They're called senior move managers, and for many families they are the difference between a move and a meltdown.

## What a senior move manager actually does

A senior move manager is a professional who plans and physically manages the downsizing and relocation of an older adult. This is not just a moving company with a nicer name. The job covers the whole arc:

- **Sorting a lifetime of belongings.** Working room by room, at your parent's pace, deciding what goes to the new home, what goes to family, what gets sold or donated, and what gets discarded. This is the longest and most emotional part of any senior move — and the part families most underestimate.
- **Floor-planning the new room before moving day.** A good move manager measures the new apartment or room and creates a to-scale floor plan, so decisions about which bed, which dresser, and which chair get made in advance. Nothing arrives that won't fit, and nothing beloved gets left behind because "we ran out of space" in a panic at 4 p.m.
- **Packing and unpacking.** They pack, supervise or arrange the movers, and then — crucially — unpack everything on the other end.
- **Setting up the new home to feel familiar.** The best move managers photograph how things were arranged in the old house and recreate it: the same pictures grouped the same way, dishes in the cabinet in the same order, the bed made with the same quilt. For a parent with memory loss especially, this continuity is not decoration — it's orientation.
- **Dispersing everything that doesn't go.** Coordinating shipments to family members, estate sales or consignment, donation pickups, and cleanout of what remains, often leaving the house broom-clean and ready to sell.

Most work through the **National Association of Senior & Specialty Move Managers (NASMM)**, the profession's trade association. NASMM members are required to carry general liability insurance and agree to a code of ethics, and the association has a standards process behind them — which matters when strangers are handling everything your parent owns.

## What it costs

Senior move managers typically charge by the hour, commonly in the range of **$60-125/hour** depending on region, with some quoting flat project fees. A full-service move — sorting, floor plan, packing, move oversight, unpacking, and setup — often lands somewhere around **$2,500-6,000**, more for large houses, long distances, or extensive dispersal work. Treat these as typical national ranges, not quotes; costs vary a lot by market, and the actual movers and truck are usually billed separately.

That's real money on top of everything else you're paying for. Weigh it against what it replaces: weeks of your own time (or repeated cross-country flights), storage-unit rent for things nobody decided about, and — this is the quiet one — the family arguments that a neutral third party prevents. Some of the dispersal work can also offset cost if the manager runs a well-handled estate sale.

## When they're worth it

Not every move needs one. A nearby family with time, a small apartment to empty, and an easygoing parent can do this themselves. A move manager earns their fee when:

- **The children live far away.** Managing a downsizing by phone from another state is miserable and slow. Our guide to [caring from a distance](/guides/caring-from-a-distance/) covers the broader challenge; a move manager solves this specific piece of it.
- **The house is big or very full.** Forty years in a four-bedroom house with an attic is a multi-week professional project, not a series of weekends.
- **Your parent is overwhelmed or resistant.** Sorting possessions is where moves stall for months. A patient professional who does this every week keeps it moving without steamrolling anyone — and parents often accept guidance from a professional that they'd fight from their own child, the same dynamic covered in [talking to a parent about care](/guides/talking-to-a-parent-about-care/).
- **Siblings are already tense about belongings.** More on this below, because it's the part nobody says out loud.

## The emotional truth: the sorting is the hard part

Here is what insiders know: the physical move is the easy 20 percent. The hard 80 percent is deciding what happens to the belongings — because the belongings are not really belongings. The china is a marriage. The workbench is a father's identity. And when adult children start dividing them, decades-old sibling dynamics come roaring back. Families that sailed through choosing a community can fracture over a dining set.

A neutral professional changes that chemistry. The move manager has no stake in who gets the clock. They keep the process moving, document decisions, and give your parent — not the loudest sibling — the final say over their own things. Many families later say the fee was worth it for the peace alone. If the whole transition is stirring up grief and conflict, our guide to [coping with the decision](/guides/coping-with-the-decision/) pairs well with this one.

> **Ask this:** Before hiring anyone, ask your parent: "Which five things in this house matter most to you?" Whatever they name goes on the floor plan first. Starting from what stays — instead of what goes — turns downsizing from a series of losses into a set of choices.

## How to find one

Start with the **NASMM directory** at nasmm.org, which lets you search members by location. You can also ask the community your parent is moving into — move-in coordinators work with local move managers constantly and know who is good (just remember a recommendation is a starting point, not a vetting). If you're still choosing the community itself, work through [how to choose a senior care facility](/guides/how-to-choose-a-senior-care-facility/) first, since the destination shapes the whole move plan.

Interview two or three. Any good one will do a free or low-cost initial consultation and give you a written estimate afterward.

## Vetting questions

Ask every candidate:

- Are you a NASMM member, and do you carry general liability insurance? Ask for the certificate — a real professional expects this question.
- Will you give me a **written estimate** with the hourly rate, estimated hours, and what's billed separately (movers, supplies, hauling, estate-sale commissions)?
- Can I speak to two or three recent client families as references?
- Have you handled moves into this specific community — and do you create a to-scale floor plan before moving day?
- How do you handle it when family members disagree about an item?
- If items are sold, how are proceeds documented and returned?

> **Ask this:** "Walk me through your last difficult move — what went wrong and how did you handle it?" Anyone who says nothing ever goes wrong hasn't done many moves, or isn't being straight with you. The honest answer tells you how they'll handle yours.

One honest caution: unlike placement agents, move managers are generally paid by you, not by facilities — which keeps their incentives clean. But the title isn't legally protected, and anyone can print "senior move specialist" on a business card. Insurance, references, and a written estimate are your protection; skip any candidate who resists providing all three.

## After the hiring: connect them to move-in day

If you hire a move manager, loop them in on the community's move-in rules early — elevator reservations, prohibited items, and whether the room can be set up the day before. Then use our [move-in day guide](/guides/move-in-day-guide/) for the human side of the day: what to bring, the first hour in the room, and the goodbye. The move manager handles the boxes; that guide helps you handle the feelings.

## Common questions

**Is a senior move manager the same as a placement agent?**
No. A placement agent helps you pick a community (and is usually paid a commission by the facility). A senior move manager handles the downsizing and the physical move, and is paid by you. Some families use both; they solve different problems.

**Does [Medicare](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/) or insurance pay for a senior move manager?**
Generally no — this is an out-of-pocket expense. Occasionally long-term care insurance or veterans' programs offer limited relocation help, and some communities run move-in specials that offset costs; it never hurts to ask, but budget as if you're paying yourself.

**How far in advance should we book?**
For a full downsizing, contact them four to eight weeks before the move date if you can — the sorting phase is what takes time. Good ones can also mobilize fast after a hospital discharge or a sudden opening on a waitlist, so call even if your timeline is short.

**What happens to everything that doesn't move?**
The move manager coordinates the dispersal: shipping items to family, consignment or estate sale for things with value, donation pickups for the rest, and haul-away of what remains. Ask up front how proceeds and receipts are documented.
