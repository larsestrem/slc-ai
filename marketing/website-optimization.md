Advertising gets people to your website. Your website decides what happens next. Whether the traffic comes from a [Google Ad Grant](/marketing/ad-grants/), paid search, or organic results, every visitor lands on the same pages - and those pages either turn a worried adult daughter into a scheduled tour, or they quietly send her to the community down the road.

We run senior.living.community, a family-facing directory, which means we spend all day watching how families actually research senior care. This page describes what we look for when we audit a community's website, what we fix, and how the engagement works.

## Why the website is the multiplier

Suppose your campaigns deliver **2,000** targeted visits a month. If your site converts **1%** of visitors into tour requests, that's **20** tours. If it converts **2%**, that's **40** - from the exact same traffic and the exact same ad spend.

Doubling your conversion rate has the same effect on tours as doubling your advertising budget, and it costs far less. It also compounds: every future visitor, from every future channel, lands on the improved site. That's why we treat the website as the first thing to inspect, not an afterthought to the campaigns.

## Who is actually visiting

The typical visitor is not the future resident. It's an adult son or daughter, often in their 50s or 60s, researching on a phone between work and family obligations, frequently after a fall, a diagnosis, or a hospital discharge started the clock. She is stressed, short on time, and comparing three or four communities in the same session.

Every audit item below follows from that reality.

## What we audit and fix

### Page speed on real phones

Most community websites are tested - if at all - on an office desktop with fast Wi-Fi. Your actual visitor is on a mid-range phone, sometimes on a cellular connection in a hospital hallway. Pages that take five or more seconds to load lose a meaningful share of visitors before a single word is read, and both Google's organic ranking and its ad quality scoring reward fast pages. We measure speed the way Google does, on the devices your visitors use, and fix the usual culprits: oversized images, bloated themes, and plugins nobody remembers installing.

### Clear care-level explanations

Families arrive confused. Most don't yet know the difference between assisted living, memory care, and skilled nursing - they know "Mom can't be alone anymore." A site that assumes industry vocabulary loses them; a site that plainly explains what each level of care includes, who it's for, and how a care assessment works becomes the site they trust. We rewrite care-level pages in family language, without dumbing down the substance.

### Pricing transparency vs. the "call us" wall

The most common conversion killer in this industry is hiding all pricing behind "contact us for pricing." Families read that as "more than we can afford" and leave - often for a competitor who publishes a starting rate. You don't need to publish a full rate card; a starting price, a typical range, or an honest explanation of how pricing is built (base rate plus care level) keeps the visitor in the conversation. Communities that publish even a starting rate consistently see more inquiries, not fewer - and the inquiries that come in are better qualified.

### Tour-request friction

We walk the exact path a visitor takes to request a tour and count every obstacle: forms with **10+** required fields, no visible phone number, no indication of what happens after you submit, contact pages buried three clicks deep. The fix is usually simple - a short form, a prominent phone number, a one-line promise ("we'll call you within one business day") - but someone has to actually do it.

### Photos that answer real questions

Families zoom in on photos looking for specific answers: How big are the rooms? Is it clean? Do the residents look engaged? Is there space for Dad's recliner? Stock photography and empty-lobby shots answer none of that. We identify the photo gaps and give you a specific shot list - real rooms, real common areas, real activities (with appropriate permissions).

### Trust signals

State inspection records are public; families increasingly check them. A community that links to its own inspection history, names its leadership, mentions staff tenure ("our director of nursing has been here **11** years"), and shows genuine reviews signals it has nothing to hide - which is exactly the signal a nervous family is scanning for. We audit what trust evidence exists, what's missing, and how to present it honestly.

### Local SEO and AEO

Local SEO is the established discipline: an accurate and active Google Business Profile, consistent name-address-phone data, location pages that actually describe your location. AEO - answer-engine optimization - is where discovery is moving. Families now ask ChatGPT and Google's AI results questions like "what does assisted living cost near me" and "how do I choose a memory care facility." AI assistants and featured snippets cite pages that are structured to be citable: clear headings that match real questions, direct answers near the top, structured data markup that machines can parse. We optimize for both, because the family that finds you through an AI answer never saw a search results page at all.

## What each problem costs you

| What we find | What the family experiences | The fix |
|---|---|---|
| Slow mobile pages | Leaves before the page loads | Image, theme, and hosting cleanup |
| Jargon-heavy care pages | "I don't know if this is the right place for Mom" | Plain-language care-level content |
| No pricing information | "Probably too expensive" - leaves | Starting rates or honest ranges |
| Long, buried contact forms | Gives up mid-form | Short form, visible phone, clear next step |
| Stock photos | Can't picture their parent living there | Shot list of real spaces and life |
| No trust evidence | Keeps researching competitors | Inspection links, tenure, real reviews |
| No structured content | Never surfaces in AI answers | AEO restructuring and markup |

## When optimization isn't the answer

Sometimes the audit comes back and the right recommendation is: don't patch this. If the site is built on a decade-old theme, structured around the org chart instead of the family's questions, and slow at the platform level, optimization hits a ceiling - you can spend real money polishing something that will never score well. In that case we'll say so plainly and point you at the [website rebuild](/marketing/website-rebuild/) option instead. And if your site is genuinely in good shape, we'll tell you that too, and suggest you put the budget into [traffic](/marketing/) instead.
