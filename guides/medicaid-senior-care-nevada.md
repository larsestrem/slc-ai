If your parent is in Nevada and the money for care is running out, you need straight answers fast. Here they are: Nevada Medicaid reliably pays for nursing [home care](/guides/in-home-care-explained/) for people who qualify. It can also pay for care in [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/) and group residential homes — but through a capped waiver program with limited slots, so timing and realistic expectations matter.

## What Nevada's Medicaid program is called

Nevada's program is simply **Nevada Medicaid**, run by the Division of Health Care Financing and Policy (DHCFP) within the Department of Health and Human Services. Two other agencies matter for seniors: the **Division of Welfare and Supportive Services (DWSS)** handles financial eligibility applications (through Access Nevada), and the **Aging and Disability Services Division (ADSD)** runs the home and community-based programs for older adults.

That's three acronyms, but the practical takeaway is simple: financial application through DWSS, care programs through ADSD — and they'll coordinate once you're in the system.

## Yes, it pays for nursing homes

For eligible Nevadans, Medicaid pays for care in Medicaid-certified [nursing homes](/guides/skilled-nursing/), including room and board. This is an entitlement — no waitlist — for those who meet the financial rules and need nursing-facility-level care. Most of your parent's monthly income then goes toward the cost of care, with a small personal needs allowance kept back.

Medicare is different: it covers only short rehab stays, not ongoing care. Our [Medicaid vs. Medicare guide](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/) explains where families most often get caught out.

## Assisted living and group homes: the Frail Elderly (FE) Waiver

Nevada's main program for seniors outside nursing homes is the **Home and Community Based Services Waiver for the Frail Elderly** — usually called the **FE Waiver**. It serves people 65 and older who would otherwise qualify for nursing home placement, and it can pay for care in:

- Your parent's own home or a family member's home
- **Residential facilities for groups** (Nevada's licensing term for group care homes)
- **Assisted living facilities**

In residential settings, the waiver pays for "augmented personal care" — hands-on help and supervision above the facility's baseline obligations. As in every state, the waiver covers **care services, not room and board**; housing and meals come out of your parent's own income.

Two honest realities:

- **Slots are capped.** The FE Waiver has a limited number of enrollment slots (on the order of a few thousand statewide in recent years). When slots fill, a waitlist forms. Placement off the waitlist is by priority — people leaving nursing homes and those with the heaviest care needs go first — not strictly first-come, first-served.
- **Participating facilities are limited.** Only facilities enrolled as Medicaid waiver providers can take FE Waiver residents, and they're a minority of Nevada's assisted living market, concentrated in the Las Vegas and Reno areas. Always ask a facility directly whether it's an enrolled FE Waiver provider. Confirm current provider availability with ADSD.

> **Ask this:** "Are you an enrolled Nevada Medicaid FE Waiver provider, and do you have a waiver opening now — or a realistic estimate of when one will open?"

## Who qualifies: the basic shape

Both of these must be true:

- **Care need:** your parent must be 65+ (for the FE Waiver) and assessed as needing nursing-facility-level care — substantial help with daily activities like bathing, toileting, and eating, or supervision for cognitive impairment.
- **Finances:** income and asset limits apply. A single applicant's countable assets generally must be very low (around $2,000 in recent years), with an income cap tied to federal figures that adjust annually. A spouse remaining at home keeps a significantly larger protected share of assets and income under federal spousal impoverishment rules.

Nevada applies the standard **five-year look-back**: gifts or below-market asset transfers within five years of applying can trigger a penalty period of ineligibility. Get professional advice before moving any money. Exact dollar limits change yearly — confirm current figures with DWSS or an elder law attorney rather than trusting online numbers.

## Waitlists: apply before you're desperate

Because the FE Waiver is capped, the single most useful thing you can do is apply early — even if your parent is managing today. If a waitlist exists, months can pass before a slot opens, and priority rules mean a later applicant with higher needs may be served first. Meanwhile, nursing home coverage remains available without a waitlist if care needs become urgent. Ask ADSD about the current waitlist situation when you apply — it changes.

## How to apply

- **Care programs (FE Waiver):** contact your regional **Aging and Disability Services Division (ADSD)** office — Las Vegas, Reno, Carson City, or Elko — and ask to start an intake for the Frail Elderly Waiver. Intake can often be started by phone.
- **Financial eligibility:** apply for Medicaid through the **Division of Welfare and Supportive Services**, online via Access Nevada or at a local office.
- **Local guidance:** Nevada's **Aging and Disability Resource Centers** and your local **Area Agency on Aging** resources (find them via the Eldercare Locator, 1-800-677-1116) can help you navigate both tracks.

Gather bank statements, income records, property documents, and insurance policies before you start. Applications stall on missing paperwork more than anything else.

## Estate recovery

After a Medicaid recipient dies, Nevada seeks repayment from the estate for long-term care costs it paid — and that can reach the family home. Exceptions and hardship waivers exist, and nothing is recovered while a surviving spouse is alive. It's not a reason to avoid Medicaid, but go in with eyes open: read our [Medicaid estate recovery guide](/guides/medicaid-estate-recovery/) and talk to an elder law attorney about the house before applying.

## Common questions

**Does Nevada Medicaid pay for assisted living?**
It can, through the FE Waiver, for people 65+ who meet care and financial rules — but slots are capped, a waitlist may apply, and only enrolled provider facilities participate. Room and board still comes from your parent's income.

**My dad is under 65 — is there anything for him?**
The FE Waiver is 65+, but Nevada has other programs for younger adults with physical disabilities, including personal care services at home. Ask ADSD what fits his situation.

**Is there a waitlist for nursing home coverage too?**
No. Nursing home Medicaid is an entitlement — if your parent qualifies financially and medically, it pays. The waitlist risk applies only to the waiver programs.

**Can my mom keep the house and get Medicaid?**
Often yes while she or a spouse lives in it — the home is generally exempt in that situation, subject to equity limits — but estate recovery can apply after death. This is a question for an elder law attorney before you apply, not after.

## Where to get help

- **ADSD regional offices** (adsd.nv.gov) — FE Waiver intake and aging services
- **Division of Welfare and Supportive Services / Access Nevada** — Medicaid financial applications
- **Nevada Aging and Disability Resource Centers** and **Eldercare Locator:** 1-800-677-1116
- **Nevada SHIP** (State Health Insurance Assistance Program) — free Medicare counseling
- An **elder law attorney** — for spend-down, spousal protection, and estate recovery questions
