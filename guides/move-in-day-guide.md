You found the place, signed the papers, and now there's a date on the calendar that feels enormous. Move-in day is emotional for everyone, but it goes better with a plan - for the boxes, for the paperwork, and for the goodbye. This page walks you through all three, plus the two weeks that follow, which are often harder than the day itself.

## What to bring: the short list that matters

Rooms in senior living are smaller than the closets in most family homes. The goal is not to move a household - it's to move the *feeling* of home. A handful of things do almost all of that work:

- **Their own bedding.** The familiar comforter and pillow, not new ones. The first night is strange enough; the bed should smell and feel like theirs.
- **Photos on the wall on day one.** Not in a box for later - hung or propped up before you leave. Family faces on the wall are the fastest way a room stops feeling institutional. Bring a small hammer, hooks, and removable strips.
- **The chair.** If there's one chair your parent always sat in and it fits the floor plan, bring it. Staff will tell you: the resident who has their own chair settles faster.
- **Clothing, labeled.** A few weeks' worth of comfortable, washable clothes - with their name in each item. Community laundry mixes loads, and unlabeled clothes disappear. Iron-on or stick-on labels take an evening and prevent months of frustration.
- **A current medication list**, plus the actual medications in original containers if the community is taking over med management. Bring the pharmacy's phone number too.
- **Spares of the small essentials:** a backup pair of glasses, extra hearing-aid batteries (or a spare aid if one exists), denture cups, chargers. These are the items most often lost in a move and hardest to live without.
- **A few familiar objects:** the clock they know the sound of, a favorite lamp, a couple of books, the afghan from the couch.

If you're choosing what fits a shared room versus a private one, our guide to [shared vs. private rooms](/guides/shared-vs-private-rooms/) covers how much space to actually plan for.

## What NOT to bring

This list matters as much as the first one:

- **Valuables and heirlooms.** Jewelry, cash, irreplaceable items. Communities are not banks, and many people pass through a busy building - small valuables get lost. Bring costume jewelry if jewelry matters; keep the real pieces with family.
- **Too much.** The most common move-in mistake is overfilling the room. Clutter is a fall hazard, makes housekeeping harder, and makes the room feel chaotic instead of homey. When in doubt, leave it out - you can always bring more later, and "later" almost never comes.
- **Furniture that doesn't fit the floor plan.** Measure first. Walkers and wheelchairs need clear paths. If sorting a whole house down to one room feels impossible, that's exactly the job a [senior move manager](/guides/senior-move-managers/) does professionally - including drawing the floor plan before moving day so nothing arrives that won't fit.
- **Anything the community prohibits.** Space heaters, extension cords, throw rugs, and sometimes candles or certain appliances are commonly banned for safety. Ask for the list in advance rather than getting flagged on day one.

## The first hour: make it look like home before anything else

When you arrive, resist the urge to start with paperwork or boxes of clothes. Do the visual things first: make the bed with their bedding, hang the photos, place the chair, set the lamp. This ordering matters - a parent who walks into a room that already looks like theirs has a completely different first day than one who sits amid boxes and bare walls. If you can set up the room the day before, do it. Many communities allow this; few families think to ask.

> **Ask this:** "Can we set up the room the day before move-in?" and "What's your policy on hanging things on the walls?" Communities that say yes to early setup are handing you the single best move-in advantage there is.

## Paperwork and logistics on the day

Expect to spend real time with the business office and nursing staff. Have ready: the signed agreement, photo ID, insurance and Medicare/Medicaid cards, the medication list, physician contacts, any required health assessment, and copies of legal documents (power of attorney, advance directive, POLST if there is one). Confirm who to call after hours and when the first care plan meeting happens - most communities schedule one within a few weeks. Set up mail forwarding before you leave.

## The goodbye: arrive mid-morning, leave before dinner

There is a choreography to this day that experienced staff will quietly recommend:

- **Arrive mid-morning.** It gives you a full unhurried day without the exhaustion of an early start.
- **Stay for one meal.** Lunch in the dining room together is a warm, normal way to introduce the routine - and meals are where friendships start.
- **Leave before dinner.** Let your parent have their first dinner in the community, ideally seated with residents the staff have picked as good table matches (ask them to arrange this - good communities do it automatically).
- **Keep the goodbye short and warm.** Long, tearful goodbyes make it harder for both of you. Say when you'll call and when you'll visit, hug, and go. Lingering in the doorway tells your parent something is wrong.
- **Let staff start their job.** The staff know how to welcome a new resident; it is literally their profession. Your leaving is what allows them to begin.

## The first two weeks: the adjustment curve is real

Expect complaints. Expect "the food is terrible," "I don't like it here," "come get me." This is nearly universal, and it is not proof you made a mistake - it's homesickness, and it follows a curve. For most residents, things begin to turn somewhere between weeks two and six, once routines form and a few familiar faces appear at meals and activities.

On visiting: communities genuinely differ in what they recommend. Some encourage frequent early visits to reassure; others - especially in [memory care](/guides/memory-care/) - suggest a planned short gap of several days to a couple of weeks so the resident bonds with staff and routine instead of waiting by the door. Neither is wrong. Ask what *this* community has seen work, and ask them to tell you honestly how your parent is doing when you're not there - staff usually see a more settled person than the one who performs distress on the phone.

> **Ask this:** "In the first two weeks, do you recommend frequent visits or a short break - and will you call me with honest updates either way?" The answer tells you both their philosophy and whether they'll be a real partner.

Phone calls follow the same logic. A predictable rhythm - say, a call every evening at seven - comforts more than constant check-ins, which can keep homesickness stirred up. If calls consistently end in tears, shorter and more scheduled usually beats longer and more frequent.

## The guilt that shows up that first night

You will probably drive home, sit down, and feel awful. Almost everyone does. The empty passenger seat, the voice saying *you left them there.* Hear this: that feeling is grief and love wearing a guilt costume, not evidence you did the wrong thing. You moved your parent somewhere you chose carefully so they can be safe, fed, and cared for around the clock - and you get to go back to being their child instead of their exhausted nurse. If the guilt lingers or gets loud, our guide to [coping with the decision](/guides/coping-with-the-decision/) was written for exactly this night. And if you're still second-guessing the choice itself, revisit [how to choose a senior care facility](/guides/how-to-choose-a-senior-care-facility/) - you'll likely find you did the homework right.

## Common questions

**How long does it take a parent to adjust to senior living?**
Most residents settle in somewhere between two and six weeks, though some take a few months. The turn usually comes when routines form and a friendship or two takes hold. Persistent decline, isolation, or weight loss beyond that window is worth raising with staff and the doctor.

**Should I visit every day the first week?**
It depends on the person and the community. Frequent visits reassure some residents; for others - especially in memory care - daily visits reset the homesickness clock each time. Ask the community what they've seen work and agree on a plan together.

**What if my parent begs to come home?**
Stay calm, validate the feeling ("I know this is hard"), and don't negotiate the decision in the moment. Reassure them of the next call or visit, then alert staff so they can add support. In the vast majority of cases this fades as the adjustment curve turns.

**Who physically moves the furniture?**
Some families do it themselves; many hire regular movers for the heavy items. If the move also involves emptying a family home, a [senior move manager](/guides/senior-move-managers/) can handle the sorting, packing, floor plan, and setup end to end.
