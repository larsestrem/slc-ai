Oregon has strengthened the rules governing its care facilities. Senate Bill 739, which the [Oregon Capital Chronicle](https://oregoncapitalchronicle.com/2025/06/23/oregon-senate-oks-bill-to-strengthen-care-facility-investigations-oversight/) reported passed the state Senate in June 2025 and was signed into law, tightens oversight of [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/), residential care, and [memory care](/guides/memory-care/) communities, with provisions phasing in through 2026.

As the [Salem Reporter](https://www.salemreporter.com/2025/06/23/oregon-senate-oks-bill-to-strengthen-care-facility-investigations-oversight/) described the measure, the law is aimed at faster investigations and better communication with families. Among its provisions, the state must investigate certain abuse or neglect complaints on a tight timeline, including within 24 hours when a report involves a resident's death, and facilities must notify residents and their representatives when a violation results in actual harm. The law also requires that residents' contacts be told when a community is found to have violated state abuse and neglect policies or is placed on a state oversight watchlist, alongside stricter eligibility standards for facility administrators. The Oregon Department of Human Services has published [guidance for providers and families](https://www.oregon.gov/odhs/engagement/Documents/2026-03-sb739-mcc-faq.pdf) as the changes take effect.

## What this means for your family

The heart of this law is information: making sure families actually hear about serious problems, and quickly. For years, one of the hardest parts of choosing and monitoring care has been that families often learned about a violation or an incident late, or not at all. Oregon's changes push in the other direction, toward faster investigations of the most serious complaints and clearer notice when harm occurs.

For your family, a few practical takeaways. If you have a loved one in an Oregon community, make sure you are listed as a resident representative or emergency contact, because much of the new notice framework runs through those designated people. Ask the community directly how it handles complaints and how it will notify you if something goes wrong, and see whether the answer matches the standards the state now expects. And keep checking the primary source yourself: the state licensing record is where investigations, findings, and any watchlist status show up. Our guide to [reading a community's state record](/guides/checking-complaints-and-state-records/) explains where to look and how to weigh what you find.

Stronger rules on paper only help if families use them. Oregon is part of a national wave of states tightening assisted living and memory care oversight, but the most reliable protection is still an engaged family that asks questions, reads the record, and speaks up early. If a community is reluctant to explain how it handles concerns, treat that as the answer.
