A community can't control what families write about it online. It can control how it responds,
and that response is one of the most revealing things a prospective family will ever read.
A warm, specific, accountable reply signals a culture that takes people seriously. A defensive
or robotic one signals the opposite. This page is about doing it well, and about the one line
that must never be crossed.

## The line that overrides everything: resident privacy

Before any other rule: **a community must never reveal a resident's private health information in
a public reply.** Not their diagnosis, their care needs, their behavior, their medications, or
the details of an incident. In many cases, even confirming that a specific person was a resident
can breach their privacy. This holds whether the review came from the resident, a family member,
or a former employee, and whether or not it was fair.

It is genuinely tempting to "set the record straight" when a review feels one-sided. Resist it.
Correcting a factual claim by disclosing someone's medical situation trades a small reputational
win for a serious violation of dignity and, potentially, law. The strongest response to an unfair
review is never a rebuttal of clinical facts in public; it's an invitation to talk privately.

## What a good response does

- **Leads with acknowledgment, not defense.** "Thank you for taking the time to share this, and
  I'm sorry your experience fell short" costs nothing and changes the tone entirely.
- **Stays warm and human.** A named person (an executive director, not "the management team")
  signing a reply that sounds like a person, not a legal department.
- **Takes it offline quickly.** "I'd like to understand what happened and make it right. Please
  call me directly at..." Real resolution happens in a conversation, not a comment thread.
- **Shows a process, not a promise.** "Here's how we look into concerns like this" reassures a
  reader far more than "we pride ourselves on excellent care."
- **Answers the quiet reader.** The audience for a response is rarely the reviewer; it's the
  dozen prospective families reading months later. Write for them.
- **Thanks the good reviews too, specifically.** Generic "thanks for the 5 stars" reads as
  automated. Naming what the family mentioned shows a real person read it.

## What a poor response does

Blames the reviewer or the resident. Argues details. Sounds copy-pasted across every review.
Goes silent on the hard ones and only answers the glowing ones. Or, worst of all, reveals
something private to score a point. Any one of these tells a prospective family more than the
original complaint did.

## For families reading the responses

Weigh the response, but don't let a polished reply erase a serious pattern. A good answer to a
one-off complaint is reassuring; a good answer to repeated, substantiated safety problems is not
a fix. Read the responses alongside the [official record](/guides/checking-complaints-and-state-records/)
and with an eye for what the reply avoids saying. Our guides on
[interpreting online reviews](/guides/interpreting-online-reviews/) and
[why even good communities get complaints](/guides/why-good-communities-get-complaints/) go
deeper on weighing what you find.

## A quick before-and-after

**Weak:** "We disagree with this review. This resident required a level of care our staff worked
very hard to provide, and the family's expectations were unrealistic."
(Defensive, and it exposes the resident's care situation.)

**Strong:** "Thank you for sharing this, and I'm sorry it left you frustrated. I take this
seriously and would like to understand exactly what happened so we can do better. Please reach me
directly at [phone] or [email]. - [Name], Executive Director."
(Accountable, human, private, and it protects the resident.)

The pattern is the same every time: acknowledge, stay human, move it to a private conversation,
and never trade someone's privacy for a public point.
