If you're trying to figure out what kind of care your parent actually needs, you've probably run into a wall of confusing terms — assisted living, memory care, skilled nursing, CCRC. This page explains what each level of care really provides, who it's for, and how the costs work. Once you understand the levels, every other decision gets easier.

## Independent living

Independent living is housing, not care. Residents get an apartment or cottage, meals in a shared dining room, housekeeping, activities, and neighbors their own age. There are no nurses on staff and no help with bathing, dressing, or medications.

It's a good fit for someone who is healthy but lonely, tired of maintaining a house, or wants a built-in community. If your parent already needs daily hands-on help, independent living alone won't be enough — though many communities let you hire home care to come in.

Costs run roughly $2,500–$4,500 per month nationally, and vary a lot by region.

## Assisted living

Assisted living adds hands-on personal care: help with bathing, dressing, toileting, medication management, and getting to meals. Staff are available around the clock, but they are caregivers and med techs, not nurses providing medical treatment. Residents typically have their own apartment or room.

This is the right level for someone who can no longer manage daily tasks safely but doesn't need daily nursing care. The national median is roughly $5,500–$6,000 per month for the base rate — and the base rate is only part of the story.

### How "levels of care" inside assisted living change the bill

Here's what many families don't learn until the first bill arrives: most assisted living communities charge a base rent plus a **care level fee** based on an assessment. A nurse scores your parent on things like mobility, continence, medication needs, and cognition, then assigns a level — commonly Level 1, 2, or 3, though some communities use point systems with more tiers.

- **Level 1** might mean medication reminders and standby help — often adds $500–$1,000/month.
- **Level 2** might mean hands-on help with bathing and dressing — often adds $1,000–$2,000/month.
- **Level 3** might mean two-person transfers or incontinence care — can add $2,000–$3,000/month or more.

The insider reality: the move-in assessment is sometimes scored generously to close the sale, then re-scored higher at the 30- or 90-day review. Ask how often reassessments happen, how much each level costs, and what the notice period is for a price change.

> **Ask this:** "Can you show me the full care-level fee schedule in writing, and what would my mother's monthly total be at the next level up?"

## Memory care

Memory care is specialized care for people with Alzheimer's disease or other dementias. It usually means a secured unit or building (so residents can't wander into danger), higher staffing, dementia-trained caregivers, and structured daily engagement. Nationally, expect roughly $7,000–$8,500 per month.

Memory care can be a wing inside an assisted living community or a standalone building. See our full guide to [memory care](/guides/memory-care/) for what separates good programs from locked doors with a nicer name.

## Skilled nursing (nursing homes)

Skilled nursing facilities provide 24-hour licensed nursing care — wound care, injections, feeding tubes, complex medication management, and rehabilitation after a hospital stay. This is the only level that is truly medical.

Two very different groups live there: short-term rehab patients (often covered by Medicare for a limited time) and long-term residents who need daily nursing indefinitely. A private room runs roughly $10,000–$12,000 per month nationally; semi-private is somewhat less. Medicaid is the main payer for long-term nursing home care — see [Medicaid vs. Medicare](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/).

## CCRCs and life-plan communities

A continuing care retirement community (CCRC), also called a life-plan community, offers independent living, assisted living, and skilled nursing on one campus. You move in while independent and transfer between levels as needs change, without leaving the community.

The catch is the money: many CCRCs charge a large entrance fee — often $100,000 to $500,000 or more — plus monthly fees, under contracts that vary widely in what care they actually guarantee and what's refundable. Have an elder law attorney or fee-only financial advisor read the contract before anyone signs.

## Respite care

Respite care is a short-term stay — usually a few days to a few weeks — in an assisted living or memory care community. Families use it when a caregiver needs a break, after a hospital discharge, or as a low-risk trial run of a community you're considering. It's typically billed at a daily rate.

The trial-run use is underrated: a two-week respite stay tells you more about a community than five tours ever will.

## Hospice

Hospice is comfort-focused care for people expected to be in their final six months of life. It is a service, not a place — hospice teams come to wherever your parent lives, including assisted living and nursing homes, and it's covered by Medicare. Choosing hospice doesn't mean giving up; it means shifting the goal from cure to comfort, and families consistently say they wish they'd started it sooner.

## How needs progress over time

Most people move through levels gradually: independent living, then assisted living, then memory care or skilled nursing. But progression is rarely tidy. A single fall or hospitalization can jump someone two levels overnight.

Plan for the level your parent will need in two years, not just today. A community that offers the next level up saves you a second wrenching move later — ask directly what would trigger a required move-out, because every community has conditions it won't handle.

> **Ask this:** "What specific conditions or behaviors would require my parent to move out, and how much notice would we get?"

## Common questions

**Can my parent stay in assisted living if they develop dementia?**
Often yes, in early stages, especially if the community has a memory care wing. But wandering, exit-seeking, or aggression usually triggers a required move to memory care. Ask for the community's specific criteria in writing.

**Does Medicare pay for assisted living?**
No. Medicare covers short-term skilled nursing after a qualifying hospital stay and covers hospice, but it does not pay for assisted living, memory care, or long-term nursing home custodial care. Medicaid may help in many states — rules vary by state.

**What if we choose a level that's too low?**
The community's assessment usually catches it — they'll require a higher care level (and fee) or decline admission. The bigger risk is a too-low level masking decline; unexplained weight loss or falls often mean the care plan hasn't kept up.

**Is a CCRC worth the entrance fee?**
It can be, for people who value guaranteed access to future care and can afford it. But contract types (Type A, B, C) differ enormously in what's covered and refundable. Never sign without independent legal review of the contract and the community's financial health.

## Where to get help

- **Eldercare Locator** (1-800-677-1116) connects you to local aging services anywhere in the U.S.
- **Area Agencies on Aging** offer free help understanding care options in your county.
- **Aging and Disability Resource Centers** can arrange a needs assessment.
- Your state's **long-term care ombudsman** can answer questions about residents' rights at any licensed facility.
