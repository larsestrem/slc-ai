The residency agreement is the least-read important document in senior living: dozens of pages, signed at an emotional moment, often with a pen already in your hand. You don't need to read all of it in the office - you need to find five clauses and understand one signature trap. This page shows you exactly where to look. It's education, not legal advice; for the real thing, see the end of this page.

## First, the ground rules

Two things first. **You may take the contract home.** Any community that pressures you to sign on the spot - "this rate is only good today," "the apartment won't last" - has told you something about itself. A day's delay almost never costs you the room, and a community confident in its contract doesn't fear your kitchen table.

And **the marketing tour is not the contract.** Everything the salesperson promised - the care that's "all included," the rate that "rarely goes up" - only exists if it's in writing. What insiders know: sales staff often genuinely believe their promises, but the contract was written by corporate lawyers, and the contract wins every time. If a promise matters, ask them to point to the clause.

## Clause 1: Discharge and transfer triggers

Every agreement lists conditions under which the community can require your parent to move out - this is the clause families skip and later regret most. Look for:

- **Care-level triggers.** Most [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/) contracts say the community can discharge a resident whose needs exceed what it "can safely meet" - commonly things like being unable to transfer without two people, wandering, aggressive behaviors, or complex nursing needs. Vague language here means the community decides.
- **Financial triggers.** What happens when the money runs short? If your parent may eventually need Medicaid, get the community's Medicaid policy in writing now - many assisted living communities don't accept it, or accept it only after years of private pay. Our [Medicaid vs. Medicare guide](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/) explains why this matters early.
- **Notice period.** How many days' written notice must they give - 30 is common - and is there an appeal process? Nursing home residents have strong federal transfer-discharge protections; assisted living protections vary widely by state.

> **Ask this:** "Tell me about the last three residents who were asked to leave. What triggered it, and how much notice did the family get?" A straight answer tells you how the clause works in practice. A dodge tells you too.

## Clause 2: Rate increases

The rate on page one is the starting price, not the price. Find the language governing increases:

- **Is there any cap?** Most contracts allow increases at the community's discretion with 30-60 days' notice. Annual increases of 4-8% have been common in recent years; care-level charges can jump far more when a resident is reassessed into a higher tier.
- **Two separate dials.** Base rent and care fees usually rise independently. A community can hold rent steady and still raise your total cost sharply through care reassessments - see [what's not included and hidden fees](/guides/whats-not-included-hidden-fees/) for how the layers stack.
- **Ask for the history.** Request the community's actual increase percentages for the last three years, in writing. Past behavior is the best predictor you'll get, and reputable communities will share it.

## Clause 3: Refunds, deposits, and what happens at death

This is the uncomfortable check, and it's vital. Read what happens to money when your parent moves out - or dies:

- **The community fee** (often $2,500 to $12,000, roughly one-half to two times the monthly rent) is usually non-refundable after a short window. Confirm exactly when it stops being refundable.
- **Prorating at move-out and at death.** If your parent dies on the 3rd of the month, does the family owe the full month? Does billing stop at death, at removal of belongings, or 30 days after notice? Contracts differ enormously here, and grieving families routinely pay for empty rooms because nobody read this paragraph.
- **Security deposits and entrance fees.** For big [CCRC](/guides/ccrc-life-plan-communities/) entrance fees, refund formulas ("90% refundable," declining schedules) deserve an attorney's eyes, full stop.

It feels morbid to ask "what happens financially when my mother dies here?" Ask anyway. One awkward sentence can save thousands of dollars.

## Clause 4: Arbitration

Many agreements include a **pre-dispute arbitration clause**: by signing, you agree that any future dispute - including one over serious injury or neglect - goes to a private arbitrator instead of a jury. Arbitration is faster and quieter, which mostly benefits the facility; you give up the right to a jury trial before anything has gone wrong.

Know this: in **nursing homes**, federal rules generally prohibit requiring residents to sign an arbitration agreement **as a condition of admission** - it must be presented as voluntary, and you can typically decline or rescind it within a window. Confirm the current rules for your situation, since regulations shift. In assisted living, protections vary by state, but arbitration clauses are often negotiable there too: you can cross it out, initial it, and ask the community to counter-sign. Some will. Either way, signing away a jury trial should be a decision, not a default.

## Clause 5: The bed-hold policy

Residents go to the hospital - plan for it now. The bed-hold clause says how long the community will hold the room during a hospital or rehab stay, and at what charge:

- In private-pay assisted living, you typically keep paying full rate (sometimes minus care fees or meals) to hold the room - confirm which.
- In nursing homes, state Medicaid programs set bed-hold rules - how many paid days, and the right to return to the next available bed after. Rules vary by state; ask for the policy in writing.
- Ask what happens if a rehab stay runs long, a scenario our [skilled nursing guide](/guides/skilled-nursing/) covers.

## The signature trap: never sign as guarantor

The most consequential moment isn't a clause - it's the signature line. Contracts often ask an adult child to sign as **"responsible party,"** and buried in the definitions, that phrase can mean **personal financial guarantor**: if your parent's money runs out, the community can pursue *you*.

If you hold power of attorney, sign **"Jane Smith, as agent/POA for Mary Smith"** - on behalf of your parent, never personally. Note that federal law bars nursing homes from requiring a third-party guarantee as a condition of admission, but facilities still sue adult children over signature lines signed carelessly. If the community insists you sign personally, that's your cue to slow down and get advice.

> **Ask this:** "Does anything in this agreement make me personally liable for charges if my parent's funds run out?" Make them answer out loud, then check that the paper matches the answer.

## When to pay an attorney for an hour

An **elder law attorney** review's cost varies by attorney, market, and contract complexity - ask upfront. It's worth considering when a large entrance fee or CCRC contract is involved; when you're asked to sign as responsible party; when Medicaid is likely within a few years; or when the discharge, refund, or arbitration language confuses you. Weighed against a move costing $60,000-100,000+ a year, it's a modest cost for legal clarity. While you're being thorough, spend twenty minutes on the community's regulatory record too - our guide to [checking complaints and state records](/guides/checking-complaints-and-state-records/) shows how.

*This page is general education, not legal advice. Contract law and resident protections vary by state; an elder law attorney can review your specific agreement.*

## Common questions

**Can we negotiate a senior living contract?**
More than most families think. Communities with vacancies negotiate community fees, first-month rates, and sometimes arbitration clauses - occupancy is leverage. The core legal boilerplate rarely moves, but money terms often do, especially at month-end.

**What if the salesperson's promises aren't in the contract?**
Then, practically speaking, they don't exist. Ask for every promise that matters - rate guarantees, included care, dining credits - as a written addendum signed by the executive director, not the salesperson.

**Is a residency agreement the same as a lease?**
No. It bundles housing with care services, meals, and assessments, and it gives the community move-out powers a landlord doesn't have. That's exactly why the discharge and rate clauses deserve more attention than you'd give an apartment lease.

**We already signed. Is it too late?**
No - read it now anyway. Knowing the notice periods, bed-hold terms, and refund rules before a crisis is most of the value, and some items (like voluntary nursing home arbitration agreements) may still be rescindable within a window.

## Where to get help

- **An elder law attorney** - find one through the National Academy of Elder Law Attorneys (naela.org); ask for a flat-fee contract review.
- **Long-Term Care Ombudsman** - free advocates who know local facilities' contracts and practices; they serve assisted living as well as nursing homes in most states.
- **Your state's licensing agency** - for the rules on deposits, notice, and discharge that override any contract language.
- **Eldercare Locator: 1-800-677-1116** - connects you to your Area Agency on Aging for local legal-aid programs, which sometimes review contracts free for qualifying seniors.
