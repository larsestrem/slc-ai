If you're caring for a parent in Idaho and wondering what happens when the savings run out, here's the reassuring part: Idaho Medicaid pays for nursing [home care](/guides/in-home-care-explained/), and it also pays for care in [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/) facilities and small certified family homes through a statewide waiver. This page walks through what's covered, who qualifies, and how to apply.

## What Idaho's Medicaid program is called

Idaho's program is simply **Idaho Medicaid**, run by the **Idaho Department of Health and Welfare (IDHW)**. There's no separate branded name like some states use. For seniors needing long-term care, the relevant piece is Medicaid for the Aged, Blind, and Disabled and its long-term services and supports - which have their own financial rules and a care assessment beyond regular Medicaid health coverage.

## Yes, it pays for nursing homes

For Idahoans who qualify financially and medically, Medicaid pays for care in Medicaid-certified nursing homes - including room and board. Most of your parent's monthly income then goes toward the cost of care, with a small personal needs allowance kept back. Nursing home coverage is an entitlement: if your parent qualifies, it pays, with no waitlist.

Don't count on Medicare for this - it covers only short rehab stays after a hospitalization. Our [Medicaid vs. Medicare guide](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/) explains the distinction that surprises most families.

## Assisted living and certified family homes: the Aged & Disabled Waiver

Idaho's main program for care outside nursing homes is the **Aged & Disabled (A&D) Waiver**, a statewide Medicaid waiver for people who need nursing-facility-level care but can be served in the community. It pays for care services in:

- Your parent's own home or a family member's home
- **Certified family homes** - small state-certified homes, typically caring for a few residents in a family setting (Idaho's version of adult foster care)
- **Residential assisted living facilities** - what most people just call assisted living

Covered services include personal care help with bathing, dressing, and mobility, homemaker services, adult day health, respite, home modifications, emergency response systems, and [skilled nursing](/guides/skilled-nursing/) visits.

The standard caveat applies: the waiver pays for **care services, not room and board**. In assisted living or a certified family home, housing and meals are paid from your parent's own income - typically most of their Social Security - at rates far below private-pay pricing. And not every facility participates in Medicaid or holds a bed at the Medicaid rate, so ask each facility directly.

> **Ask this:** "Do you accept residents on Idaho's Aged & Disabled Waiver, and if my mom private-pays now and her money runs out, can she stay on Medicaid?"

An insider point worth knowing: in Idaho, certified family homes can sometimes be operated by a relative - in some circumstances a family member can become the certified provider and be paid to care for your parent. The rules are specific, so ask IDHW whether this could fit your family. Confirm details with the state.

## Who qualifies: the basic shape

Two tests, both required:

- **Care need:** an assessment must show your parent needs nursing-facility-level care - substantial help with daily activities or supervision for dementia.
- **Finances:** income and asset limits apply. A single applicant's countable assets generally must be very low (around $2,000 in recent years), and income is capped at a level tied to federal figures that change each year. A spouse remaining at home keeps a much larger protected share of the couple's assets and income under federal spousal impoverishment rules.

Idaho applies the standard **five-year look-back**: gifts or below-market transfers within five years of applying can trigger a penalty period. Don't move assets around first - talk to an elder law attorney. Exact dollar limits change annually, so confirm current figures with IDHW rather than relying on numbers found online.

## Waitlists and capacity

The A&D Waiver is not a legal entitlement - enrollment can be limited by funding, and if a waitlist forms, access is generally based on application date. In practice Idaho has served most eligible applicants in recent years, but capacity can change with state budgets, so confirm the current situation with IDHW when you apply. The more common practical constraint is finding a participating facility or certified family home with an opening, especially outside the Boise, Coeur d'Alene, and Idaho Falls areas.

## How to apply

- Apply through the **Idaho Department of Health and Welfare**: online at idalink.idaho.gov, by phone at 1-877-456-1233, or at a local field office.
- Say explicitly that you're applying for **long-term care / Aged & Disabled Waiver services** - this triggers both the financial review and the care assessment.
- Your local **Area Agency on Aging** (Idaho has six, covering every county) can help you understand options, and offers caregiver support and ombudsman services. Find yours through the Idaho Commission on Aging or the Eldercare Locator, 1-800-677-1116.

Before applying, gather several years of bank statements, income records, property documents, life insurance policies, and any trust paperwork. Incomplete financial documentation is the most common reason applications drag on.

## Estate recovery

After a Medicaid recipient dies, Idaho seeks repayment from the estate for long-term care costs the program paid - and Idaho's recovery program actively pursues claims, including against the family home, after both spouses have died. Exceptions and hardship provisions exist. This isn't a reason to avoid Medicaid, but it is a reason to plan: read our [Medicaid estate recovery guide](/guides/medicaid-estate-recovery/) and talk with an elder law attorney about the house before you apply.

## Common questions

**Does Idaho Medicaid pay for assisted living?**
Yes - the Aged & Disabled Waiver pays for care services in participating residential assisted living facilities statewide for people who meet care and financial rules. Room and board still comes from your parent's income, and not all facilities participate.

**What's a certified family home?**
A small, state-certified home where a caregiver provides room, board, and care for a few adults in a residential setting. Medicaid's A&D Waiver covers care in these homes, and they're often the most affordable and personal option in Idaho's smaller towns.

**Can my mom qualify if her income is over the limit?**
Possibly. Idaho uses income rules that may allow options such as a qualified income trust (Miller trust) arrangement for applicants over the cap - confirm with IDHW or an elder law attorney, because this must be set up correctly.

**How long does approval take?**
Plan on 45 days or more for a complete application, plus scheduling the care assessment. Starting before a crisis - ideally months before the money runs out - makes everything less painful.

## Where to get help

- **Idaho Department of Health and Welfare:** 1-877-456-1233 or idalink.idaho.gov - applications and assessments
- **Idaho's Area Agencies on Aging** (via the Idaho Commission on Aging) - local options counseling, caregiver support, ombudsman
- **SHIBA Idaho** (the state's SHIP program, through the Department of Insurance) - free Medicare counseling
- **Eldercare Locator:** 1-800-677-1116
- An **elder law attorney** - for spend-down, spousal protection, Miller trusts, and estate recovery planning

Rules and dollar limits change - always verify current details with the state before acting.
