Older adults are among the most vulnerable people in any emergency. A power outage that's an
inconvenience for a healthy adult can be dangerous for someone on oxygen or refrigerated
medication; a heat wave or a fast-moving wildfire is a genuine threat. In Oregon, Washington,
Idaho, and Hawaii, communities face wildfire and smoke, extreme heat, winter storms, floods, and
in the islands, hurricanes and tsunami zones. A prepared community has thought all of this through
before it happens. This is one of the clearest tests of whether a community truly puts residents
first, and one families rarely think to ask about.

## Why this matters more in senior living

Many residents can't quickly evacuate on their own, depend on medications and medical equipment,
and are more affected by heat, cold, and air quality. That means a community's plan can't just be
"call 911 and leave." It has to account for moving or sheltering frail people safely, keeping care
going without power, and doing it with whatever staff are on hand at 3 a.m. on a holiday weekend.

## What a genuinely prepared community has

- **A written, specific, current plan.** Not a generic binder, but a plan for the hazards this
  building actually faces, reviewed and updated, with clear roles for who does what.
- **Backup power that matches the need.** A generator that runs long enough to cover refrigerated
  medications, medical equipment, heat or cooling, and elevators, and that is tested regularly, not
  assumed to work.
- **Real evacuation logistics.** Where residents go, how they get there safely, how medications and
  medical records travel with them, and agreements with sister communities or facilities to receive
  them. A plan that can't actually move people isn't a plan.
- **Staff who are trained and drilled.** Everyone knows their role, and the community practices,
  including on nights and weekends when staffing is thinnest. Drills are where a paper plan meets
  reality.
- **Supplies on hand.** Several days of water, food, and essential medical supplies, backup oxygen,
  flashlights, and a way to communicate when phones or internet go down.
- **Air-quality readiness.** In wildfire country, that means filtration and a clean-air plan for
  smoke days, when going outside isn't safe for frail lungs.
- **A family communication plan.** A prepared community can tell families quickly what's happening
  and whether their parent is safe, which is exactly when families most need to hear it. See
  [keeping families informed](/best-practices/family-communication/).

## What families can ask

- **"What's your plan for a wildfire or evacuation, and where would residents go?"** A confident,
  specific answer is the point; hesitation is the flag.
- **"Do you have a backup generator, what does it power, and how often is it tested?"** "We have a
  generator" is not the same as "it runs the medication fridge and the memory-care AC for three
  days, and we test it monthly."
- **"How do you keep residents safe on smoke days or during a heat wave?"**
- **"How would you reach me, and how fast, if something happened?"**
- **"When did you last run a drill, including at night?"**

You don't need to become an emergency-management expert. You're listening for whether the answers
are specific and practiced, or vague and improvised.

## For communities

Preparedness is quiet until the day it isn't, and then it is everything. Most states require an
emergency plan, but the communities that stand out treat it as a living practice, drilled and
resourced, rather than a compliance document. Being able to answer a worried family's questions
plainly, because the plan is real, is both the right thing and a genuine mark of quality. Follow
your state and local requirements and coordinate with local emergency services; this page describes
what strong preparedness looks like in general and is not emergency-management or legal advice.
