If you're staring at California care prices and your parent's savings, wondering how this math can possibly work, this page is for you. California's Medicaid program does pay for long-term care — reliably for nursing homes, and in a much more limited way for [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/). Here's the honest picture.

## What California's Medicaid program is called

California's Medicaid program is **Medi-Cal**, run by the Department of Health Care Services (DHCS) and administered day-to-day through county social services offices. If a hospital discharge planner or facility mentions "Medi-Cal long-term care," they mean the part of Medi-Cal that pays for nursing [home care](/guides/in-home-care-explained/) and related services — which has its own rules beyond regular Medi-Cal health coverage.

## Yes, Medi-Cal pays for nursing homes

For Californians who qualify financially and medically, Medi-Cal pays for care in Medicaid-certified [skilled nursing](/guides/skilled-nursing/) facilities — including room and board. Most of your parent's monthly income then goes to the facility as their "share of cost," with a small personal needs allowance kept back. This is the workhorse of Medi-Cal long-term care, and unlike the assisted living program below, it is an entitlement: if your parent qualifies, it pays.

Medicare, by contrast, only covers short rehab stays. Our [Medicaid vs. Medicare guide](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/) explains the difference families most often get burned by.

## Assisted living: the Assisted Living Waiver — real, but limited

California does have a Medi-Cal program that pays for care in assisted living: the **Assisted Living Waiver (ALW)**. It covers care and supervision services in participating assisted living facilities (RCFEs) and public subsidized housing settings, for people who would otherwise qualify for nursing home care. Your parent's own income still pays room and board.

Now the uncomfortable truths, stated plainly:

- **It's not statewide.** The ALW operates in roughly 15 counties — including Los Angeles, San Diego, Orange, Riverside, San Bernardino, Sacramento, Alameda, Santa Clara, San Francisco, and a handful of others. If your parent lives outside a participating county, the waiver isn't available there. Confirm the current county list with DHCS.
- **There's a long waitlist.** Enrollment is capped, and the waitlist has run into the thousands — state data in late 2025 showed well over ten thousand people waiting, with waits commonly ranging from months to a couple of years. Priority goes to people transitioning out of nursing homes and certain protective-services referrals.
- **Few facilities participate.** Even in covered counties, only a minority of assisted living communities accept the ALW, and some limit how many waiver residents they'll take.

Separately, under California's **CalAIM** initiative, some Medi-Cal managed care plans offer "community supports" that can help members move from nursing homes into assisted living settings. Availability varies by plan and county — ask your parent's Medi-Cal managed care plan directly, and treat anything they promise verbally as unconfirmed until it's in writing.

> **Ask this:** "Is this facility an enrolled Assisted Living Waiver provider, and how many ALW residents do you actually have today?"

## Who qualifies: the basic shape — and a big 2026 change

Medi-Cal long-term care eligibility looks at care needs and finances:

- **Care need:** for nursing home coverage or the ALW, your parent must need a nursing-facility level of care, determined by assessment.
- **Finances:** California briefly became the only state with **no asset limit** (2024–2025). That ended: as of **January 1, 2026**, the asset limit is back — $130,000 in countable assets for an individual (higher for couples), per DHCS. Income rules also apply, and a spouse at home keeps additional protected assets and income under spousal impoverishment rules.

California's transfer and look-back rules have historically differed from other states' standard five-year look-back, and transfers made during the no-asset-limit period (2024–2025) are not supposed to count against applicants. This area is genuinely complicated and in flux — do not act on look-back assumptions without talking to a California elder law attorney, and confirm current rules with the county Medi-Cal office.

## Waitlists and capacity: plan for the wait

For nursing home coverage there's no waitlist — it's an entitlement. For the ALW, assume a long wait and build a plan B: in-home supports through Medi-Cal (including In-Home Supportive Services, IHSS), family help, or a facility your parent can afford privately while waiting. Getting on the ALW waitlist early costs nothing — do it as soon as your parent is plausibly eligible.

## How to apply

- **Medi-Cal itself:** apply through your **county social services office**, online via BenefitsCal, or by phone. For long-term care coverage, tell them explicitly it's a long-term care application.
- **Assisted Living Waiver:** enrollment goes through DHCS-contracted **Care Coordination Agencies** in participating counties — DHCS's ALW webpage lists them. The agency handles the assessment and waitlist placement.
- **Local guidance:** your local **Area Agency on Aging** (find it via the Eldercare Locator, 1-800-677-1116) can help you understand options and connect to county programs.

Gather bank statements, income records, property documents, and insurance policies before you start — the financial review is detailed, and missing paperwork is the number one cause of delays.

## Estate recovery: narrower in California, but real

California's estate recovery program is more limited than most states — in general it can only recover from assets that go through probate, which is why many California families use living trusts. But recovery for long-term care costs is real, and the rules have exceptions and edge cases. Read our [Medicaid estate recovery guide](/guides/medicaid-estate-recovery/) and confirm your family's situation with an elder law attorney before assuming the house is safe — or lost.

## Common questions

**Does Medi-Cal pay for assisted living everywhere in California?**
No. The Assisted Living Waiver operates only in certain counties, has capped enrollment with a long waitlist, and only some facilities participate. Nursing home coverage, by contrast, is available statewide to those who qualify.

**My mom was told Medi-Cal has no asset limit. Is that still true?**
No — that was true only in 2024 and 2025. As of January 1, 2026, an asset limit (around $130,000 for an individual) is back for long-term care and related programs. Confirm current figures with the county before applying.

**Does Medi-Cal pay family members to provide care at home?**
Often yes, through In-Home Supportive Services (IHSS), which can pay family caregivers for eligible recipients. It's a county-run program worth asking about while you wait on other options.

**Can we just move my dad to a county where the waiver operates?**
People do, but think it through: he'd still face the waitlist, facility participation is limited, and moving someone with dementia is disruptive. Talk to a Care Coordination Agency in the target county first.
