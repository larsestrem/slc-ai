## 1. Industry bodies & credentials

### National Placement & Referral Alliance (NPRA) — the placement industry's trade association
- **What it is:** A national trade association for the senior placement/referral industry, founded in 2017. Members are "Agents" (people who do placements) and "Affiliates" (senior living providers and ecosystem businesses). ([npralliance.org](https://www.npralliance.org/), [Options for Senior Living explainer](https://optionsforseniorliving.com/npra-the-who-what-and-why/), [Allyson Senior Solutions explainer](https://www.allysonseniorsolutions.com/blog/what-is-national-placement-and-referral-alliance-npra))
- **Code of Ethics:** Yes — a published Code of Ethics that governs conduct of NPRA agents, affiliates, and CPRS holders when working with clients, the public, and healthcare professionals. ([npralliance.org/code-of-ethics](https://www.npralliance.org/code-of-ethics))
- **Member directory:** Yes — a public member directory and a "Find a Placement Agent" lookup. ([Member directory](https://www.npralliance.org/member-directory), [Find a Placement Agent](https://npra.memberclicks.net/find-a-placement-agent)) **[uncertain: exact search fields/coverage — page blocked automated fetch]**
- **State chapters:** Yes — chapters page lists active chapters; search excerpts confirm a **Washington chapter** and Arizona chapters, with roughly 12 chapters and ~20 more "in start-up mode" at time of the cited page. ([Chapters](https://www.npralliance.org/chapters), [WA chapter page](https://npralliance.org/content.aspx?page_id=22&club_id=584256&module_id=478359)) **[uncertain: current OR/CA/NV chapter status]**
- **Signal value:** Moderate-to-good. Membership implies a signed ethics pledge, but it is a paid trade membership, not an audit. The stronger signal is the CPRS certification below.

### CPRS — Certified Placement & Referral Specialist (NPRA's certification)
- The only certification specifically designed for placement/referral agents; earned by **passing a proctored certification exam** after meeting eligibility requirements — NPRA states it will not grant it on education/experience alone. A formal **Candidate Handbook** (2024) governs eligibility, exam, scoring, code of conduct, and maintenance requirements. ([About CPRS](https://www.npralliance.org/about-cprs), [Candidate Handbook PDF](https://www.npralliance.org/assets/docs/NPRA%20Certification%20Handbook%20FINAL%202024.pdf), [Become CPRS Certified](https://npra.clubexpress.com/become-cprs-certified))
- **Signal value:** Strongest placement-specific credential available. **[uncertain: exact eligibility criteria (years of experience, etc.) — handbook not fetched; verify before publishing specifics]**

### CSA — Certified Senior Advisor (Society of Certified Senior Advisors)
- Requirements: pass a 115-question exam within 12 months of enrollment; pass a "Roles, Rules and Responsibilities" ethics exam; **pass a background check**; sign a Code of Professional Responsibility; 30 hours CE per 3 years incl. 3 hours ethics; annual attestation/renewal (~$180/yr). ([csa.us certification requirements](https://www.csa.us/certification/certification-requirements/), [FINRA designation listing](https://www.finra.org/investors/professional-designations/csa))
- **Signal value:** Legitimate (FINRA-listed, background-checked, CE-based) but **generic** — it covers aging broadly and is held by financial advisors, home-care operators, etc. It signals training and a clean background, not placement competence. Treat as supporting, not primary.

### State associations (strong regional signals)
- **OSRAA — Oregon Senior Referral Agency Association:** membership requires **minimum 2 years in business**, **registration with the State of Oregon + state criminal background check before applying**, proof of professional liability insurance, attendance at 3 meetings before board approval, and adherence to a Code of Ethics / Best Practice Standards. Public member listing on its site. ([About OSRAA](https://osraa.com/about-osraa/), [Membership application](https://osraa.com/membership-application/), [Members page](https://osraa.com/members-page/), [Bylaws PDF](https://osraa.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/2024-Oregon-Senior-Referral-Agency-Association.pdf))
- **ASRP of Washington — Association of Senior Referral Professionals:** nonprofit; membership conditioned on **compliance with RCW 18.330** and its Code of Ethics. ([asrpwa.org](https://asrpwa.org/), [Membership](https://www.asrpwa.org/membership/), [Their RCW 18.330 explainer](https://asrpwa.org/posts/elder-and-vulnerable-adult-referral-agency-act))
- **Signal value:** High for OR/WA — these bake in the state-law compliance checks you'd otherwise do yourself.

### Badges to discount (pay-to-play or self-referential)
- "Best of Senior Living"-style awards from lead-gen platforms: a 2024 Washington Post analysis found A Place for Mom's reviews/awards coexisted with care citations at recommended facilities (37.5% of 863 top-recommended facilities cited for substandard care in prior 2 years). ([Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2024/05/16/place-for-mom-assisted-living-referral/), [Senior Housing News summary](https://seniorhousingnews.com/2024/05/16/washington-post-analysis-links-a-place-for-mom-reviews-awards-with-care-citations/), [McKnight's](https://www.mcknightsseniorliving.com/news/senior-living-referral-site-accused-of-using-manipulated-reviews-listing-communities-providing-substandard-care/))
- Generic "senior care directory" badges that any advertiser receives. No third-party audit behind them.

## 2. State regulation (OR, WA, CA, NV, ID)

### Washington — regulated by disclosure statute, NO state registry
**Elder and Vulnerable Adult Referral Agency Act, RCW 18.330** (applies since Jan 1, 2012). ([Full chapter](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=18.330&full=true), [Justia mirror](https://law.justia.com/codes/washington/title-18/chapter-18-330/))
- **Disclosure statement (RCW 18.330.050):** must be given to each client **before any referral**, with client (or representative) **acknowledgment** (signature, e-signature, fax confirmation, or documented refusal); agency retains a copy. Must state the **fee amount if paid by the client**, or, if paid by the provider, **the method of computing the fee and the time/method of payment**, plus the agency's **refund policy** consistent with RCW 18.330.090. ([RCW 18.330.050](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=18.330.050), [RCW 18.330.090](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx))
- **Insurance:** combined general/professional liability coverage of **$1 million** required.
- **Records:** retain disclosure/agreement records **6 years**; clients get access on request. ([RCW 18.330.040 via LegalFix](https://www.legalfix.com/statutes/state-codes/washington/title-18/chapter-18-330/section-18-330-040))
- **Fees:** no fee may be charged/accepted from client or provider unless the agency substantially complies with the chapter; certain remuneration for referrals is prohibited (RCW 18.330.130). ([Justia 18.330.130](https://law.justia.com/codes/washington/title-18/chapter-18-330/section-18-330-130/))
- **Enforcement:** violations are per-se unfair/deceptive acts under WA's **Consumer Protection Act (ch. 19.86 RCW)** — private suits and AG enforcement. **No state license or registration exists**, so there is no WA registry to look an agency up in. ([RCW 18.330.140](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=18.330.140))
- **What your site can check:** ask for the agency's blank disclosure statement + certificate of insurance; check WA AG complaint history; ASRP membership.

### Oregon — regulated WITH a public registry (strongest verification of the five states)
**HB 2661 (2017)**, effective **July 1, 2018** — note: this is a 2017 law, not 2021 as hypothesized in the brief. ([Oregon Laws 2017 ch. 656 PDF](https://www.oregonlegislature.gov/bills_laws/lawsstatutes/2017orlaw0656.pdf), [Golden Placements explainer](https://goldenplacements.com/hb-2661-legislation-for-senior-referral-businesses/), [OSRAA regulation page](https://osraa.com/regulation-referral-placement-agencies/))
- Requirements: **registration with Oregon DHS**, **criminal background check** for all referral agents, **mandatory reporter** status, **$1M general liability insurance**, and a **conspicuous, plain-language written disclosure before any compensated referral** covering: services provided, length of any contract with the facility, agent contact info, privacy policy, whether the agent refers only to contracted facilities, and whether the facility pays the referral fee.
- **Public registry:** the **Long-Term Care Referral Registry** at [ltcr.oregon.gov](https://ltcr.oregon.gov/) has a public search of registered agents at [ltcr.oregon.gov/Agents](https://ltcr.oregon.gov/Agents). Registration is valid 2 years and must be renewed. ODHS explicitly does not endorse registrants. ([FAQ](https://ltcr.oregon.gov/FAQ), [Consumer resources](https://ltcr.oregon.gov/ConsumerResources))
- **What your site can check:** live registration status by name — this is a hard, free, public check.

### California — effectively unregulated for assisted-living (RCFE) referrals
- Agencies referring to **skilled nursing/intermediate care** must be licensed by the CA Dept. of Public Health, but this **does not apply to RCFE-only referral agencies** — i.e., typical senior living placement is unlicensed. ([Consumer Federation of California, SB 648 page](https://consumercal.org/sb-648-cfc-sponsors-bill-to-protect-seniors-from-unethical-long-term-care-referral-agencies/))
- **SB 648** (would have licensed RCFE referral agencies, required disclosure of most recent facility tour date) was **vetoed by Gov. Brown in 2016**. ([Bill status](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billStatusClient.xhtml?bill_id=201520160SB648), [CalMatters tracker](https://calmatters.digitaldemocracy.org/bills/ca_201520160sb648))
- **SB 875 (2023–24)** (DSS license for RCFE referral agencies; ban on facilities paying unlicensed agencies; disclosure + 3-year record retention; ban on holding client POA/property) **died — "Engrossed–Dead" after the June 2024 hearing was canceled**. ([Bill text/nav](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202320240SB875), [CalMatters tracker](https://calmatters.digitaldemocracy.org/bills/ca_202320240sb875))
- **What your site can check:** nothing state-specific for placement itself. Fall back on business registration (CA SOS), insurance certificates, NPRA/CPRS, BBB/AG complaints, and your own disclosure requirements.

### Nevada — newly regulated; regime in transition
- **SB 260 (2023)**, effective **Oct 1, 2023**, created the "senior living community referral agency" category in NRS ch. 449 with **pre-referral disclosure-and-consent requirements (NRS 449.114)** — contents, record maintenance, copy on request — and **conduct/compensation rules (NRS 449.1145)**. ([Enrolled bill PDF](https://legiscan.com/NV/text/SB260/id/2817803/Nevada-2023-SB260-Enrolled.pdf), [NRS ch. 449](https://www.leg.state.nv.us/nrs/nrs-449.html), [LegiScan](https://legiscan.com/NV/bill/SB260/2023))
- **[uncertain — verify]:** sources conflict on whether SB 260 required a State Board of Health **license** or allowed operation without one; **2025 legislation** then moved oversight to **registration with the Division of Public and Behavioral Health (DPBH)**, with proposed fee caps (~$2,708 initial / ~$1,354 renewal) and a transition window during which agencies may operate without license/certificate until 120 days after new regulations are adopted. ([Citizen Portal summary](https://citizenportal.ai/articles/6573688/Nevada-bill-would-move-oversight-of-senior-living-referral-agencies-to-DHHS-cap-fees), [2025 Statutes of Nevada](https://www.leg.state.nv.us/Statutes/83rd2025/Stats202510.html)) Confirm current registration status directly with DPBH before publishing NV verification claims.
- **What your site can check:** DPBH registration (once regulations finalize); the NRS 449.114 disclosure document.

### Idaho — no placement-agency law found
- Idaho licenses **facilities** (Residential Assisted Living Facilities under Idaho Code Title 39 ch. 33 / IDAPA 16.03.22) but I found **no Idaho statute or rule regulating placement/referral agencies**. ([IDHW RALF licensing](https://healthandwelfare.idaho.gov/providers/residential-assisted-living/licensing), [Idaho Code T39 ch.33](https://law.justia.com/codes/idaho/title-39/chapter-33/)) **[uncertain: absence of law is hard to prove from search; treat Idaho as "no known regulation" and note it on listings]**
- **What your site can check:** Idaho SOS business registration, insurance, credentials, references, complaints.

## 3. Reputation signals a site can actually verify

Ranked roughly by verifiability and strength:

1. **State registration where it exists** — OR: live public lookup at ltcr.oregon.gov/Agents; NV: DPBH registration (emerging). Hard, binary, free.
2. **Written pre-referral fee disclosure** — legally required in WA (RCW 18.330.050) and OR (HB 2661); require a sample from advisors in all five states. An advisor who won't produce one in CA/ID is a red flag.
3. **Liability insurance certificate ($1M)** — required in WA and OR anyway; require proof everywhere.
4. **CPRS certification / NPRA membership** — the only placement-specific credential + ethics pledge; verify via NPRA directory. CSA as a secondary, background-checked credential ([csa.us](https://www.csa.us/certification/certification-requirements/)).
5. **State association membership (OSRAA / ASRP WA)** — encodes 2-years-in-business, background check, insurance, ethics (OSRAA) and RCW 18.330 compliance (ASRP).
6. **Years in business** — verify via Secretary of State business-entity filing date (all five states have free SOS lookups) and domain/review history.
7. **Local presence vs. national call center** — verifiable by: local address, whether the advisor **tours communities with the family in person**, and whether they have personally visited the facilities they recommend (SB 648 would have mandated tour-date disclosure — a good voluntary standard to require). ([Consumer Federation of CA](https://consumercal.org/sb-648-cfc-sponsors-bill-to-protect-seniors-from-unethical-long-term-care-referral-agencies/), [Forbes/Gleckman on for-profit referral models](https://www.forbes.com/sites/howardgleckman/2024/07/03/free-for-profit-senior-services-referrals-buyer-beware/))
8. **Whether they will show options that don't pay them** — the core conflict-of-interest question. OR law already requires disclosing whether the agent refers only to contracted facilities; make that answer a published field for every listing. The WaPo/Senate scrutiny of A Place for Mom centered on paid-network-only recommendations and steering away from Medicaid-bound families. ([Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2024/05/16/place-for-mom-assisted-living-referral/), [NBC on Senate probe](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/senate-announces-probe-place-for-mom-referral-service-rcna157282), [McKnight's on Sen. Casey](https://www.mcknightsseniorliving.com/news/sen-casey-calls-out-a-place-for-mom-over-potentially-deceptive-business-practices/))
9. **Complaint history** — BBB profile (age + rating + complaint responses), state AG consumer complaint databases (WA AG is the RCW 18.330 enforcer), and court-record spot checks.
10. **Review longevity and spread** — Google reviews spanning 3+ years across multiple platforms beats a burst of recent 5-stars; the WaPo analysis is a caution against platform-hosted reviews. ([Senior Housing News](https://seniorhousingnews.com/2024/05/16/washington-post-analysis-links-a-place-for-mom-reviews-awards-with-care-citations/))

## 4. How other directories/networks vet

- **A Place for Mom** (largest): employs/contracts advisors who are paid on move-in commissions from a **paid network only** (~one month's rent per placement); does **not independently vet facility care records** — WaPo found 37.5% of 863 top-recommended facilities had substandard-care citations within 2 years; Senate Aging Committee opened a probe (2024). Advisors are largely remote/call-center; internal guidance reportedly steered advisors away from Medicaid-seeking families. This is the anti-model your directory differentiates against. ([Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2024/05/16/place-for-mom-assisted-living-referral/), [NBC](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/senate-announces-probe-place-for-mom-referral-service-rcna157282), [Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/howardgleckman/2024/07/03/free-for-profit-senior-services-referrals-buyer-beware/))
- **Franchise local-advisor networks:** CarePatrol (claims "prescreened communities," 200+ local advisors), Oasis Senior Advisors, Assisted Living Locators, Senior Care Authority ("certified" local advisors). Same facility-paid model but with in-person local advisors; vetting claims are self-made, not independently audited. ([carepatrol.com](https://carepatrol.com/), [oasissenioradvisors.com](https://www.oasissenioradvisors.com/), [assistedlivinglocators.com](https://assistedlivinglocators.com/), [seniorcareauthority.com](https://www.seniorcareauthority.com/))
- **NPRA "Find a Placement Agent"** is the closest existing vetted-ish directory (members bound by Code of Ethics; CPRS flagged). ([Find a Placement Agent](https://npra.memberclicks.net/find-a-placement-agent))
- **Gap you can fill:** no existing consumer directory combines state-registry verification + insurance proof + written fee-disclosure samples + conflict-of-interest answers on the listing itself.

## (b) Recommended published vetting checklist — "What we verify before listing an advisor"

1. **Identity & business standing** — active business registration with the state Secretary of State; we record the original filing date and require **2+ years in operation** (matching OSRAA's bar) or 2+ years verifiable placement experience for newer solo practices.
2. **State registration where required** — Oregon: current listing on the state's Long-Term Care Referral Registry (we link to it). Nevada: current DPBH registration once required. Washington: written attestation of RCW 18.330 compliance.
3. **Written fee disclosure** — we obtain the advisor's actual pre-referral disclosure form and confirm it states who pays them (community vs. family) and how the fee is computed, plus the refund policy. Required by law in WA and OR; we require it in CA, NV, and ID too.
4. **Liability insurance** — certificate of insurance showing at least $1M combined general/professional liability (the WA/OR statutory level), current.
5. **Background & credentials** — proof of state criminal background check (automatic for OR registrants; required directly elsewhere) and any credentials (CPRS, CSA) verified with the issuing body, not just claimed.
6. **Local, in-person service** — advisor is based in the market they serve, has personally toured the communities they recommend within the last 12 months, and offers to tour with families.
7. **Conflict-of-interest disclosure** — advisor answers, on the public listing: "Do you ever recommend communities that do not pay you?" and "Do you accept placement fees that vary by community?" We publish the answers verbatim.
8. **Complaint screen** — no unresolved BBB complaints, no state AG consumer-protection actions, no relevant court judgments found in the past 5 years.
9. **Reference check** — two family references from placements in the past 18 months and one professional reference (social worker, discharge planner, or community that did NOT pay them).
10. **Ethics commitment** — signed agreement to our code of conduct (modeled on the NPRA Code of Ethics and RCW 18.330/HB 2661 disclosure standards), with delisting for verified violations.

*(Points 1–5 are documentary and cheap; 6–9 are the differentiators no national platform does; publish all ten.)*

## (c) Listing-application question set for advisors

**Business basics**
1. Legal business name, DBA, state(s) of registration, and year founded. (We verify with the Secretary of State.)
2. States and metro areas you actively serve; office address in each.
3. Are you independent, or part of a franchise/network (CarePatrol, Oasis, ALL, SCA, other)? Which?
4. Number of placements completed in the last 12 months (approximate) and the settings you place into (AL, MC, adult family/foster homes, IL, SNF).

**Compliance & credentials**
5. Oregon: your Long-Term Care Referral Registry number. Nevada: DPBH registration status. Washington: confirm RCW 18.330 compliance and attach your disclosure statement template.
6. Attach your certificate of liability insurance (carrier, limits, expiration).
7. Have you and all client-facing staff passed a criminal background check in the last 3 years? Through whom?
8. Credentials held (CPRS, CSA, RN/LSW, other) with certificate numbers for verification.
9. Memberships: NPRA (chapter?), OSRAA, ASRP-WA, other. May we verify with the organization?

**Business model & conflicts**
10. Who pays you, and how is your fee computed (flat, % of first month's rent, other)? Do families ever pay? Attach the exact written disclosure a family receives, and tell us when in the process they receive it.
11. Roughly what share of communities in your market do you have referral agreements with? Will you recommend a community that does not pay you if it's the best fit? Give an example.
12. Do you accept any compensation other than placement fees from communities (marketing fees, bonuses, gifts)? Any ownership interest in any community you refer to?
13. What is your refund/fee policy if a placement fails within 30/60/90 days?

**Service model**
14. Describe your intake assessment (care needs, budget, Medicaid trajectory). Do you serve families who will need Medicaid within 24 months?
15. Do you tour communities with families in person? What share of your placements included an accompanied tour last year?
16. When did you last personally visit each community you commonly recommend? How do you check state inspection/citation history before recommending?
17. What happens after move-in — do you follow up? For how long?

**References & history**
18. Two family references from the past 18 months and one professional reference not compensated by you.
19. Any state AG complaints, lawsuits, license/registration denials or revocations, or BBB complaints in the past 5 years? Explain.
20. Links to your review profiles (Google, etc.) — we look at review age spread, not just star average.

## (d) Sourcing plan: first 25 advisors in OR/WA/CA/NV/ID

Order the states by how cheaply you can verify, and seed each metro with 3–6 advisors.

**Phase 1 — Oregon (target 6): the easy, fully verifiable win.**
- Source: cross-reference the **state registry** ([ltcr.oregon.gov/Agents](https://ltcr.oregon.gov/Agents)) with the **OSRAA member list** ([osraa.com/members-page](https://osraa.com/members-page/), [new members directory](https://osraa.com/new-members-directory/)). Anyone on both has already passed registration, background check, insurance, and a 2-year/ethics bar.
- Metros: Portland (3), Salem (1), Eugene (1), Bend/Medford (1).
- Also: attend/contact an OSRAA meeting — the board can bulk-introduce you.

**Phase 2 — Washington (target 6).**
- Source: **ASRP of Washington** members ([asrpwa.org](https://asrpwa.org/)) and the **NPRA Washington chapter** roster; fill gaps with NPRA's Find-a-Placement-Agent filtered to WA.
- Metros: Seattle/Eastside (3), Tacoma/Pierce (1), Spokane (1), Vancouver WA (1 — can double-cover Portland).
- Verification: since WA has no registry, require the RCW 18.330 disclosure template + insurance cert up front.

**Phase 3 — California (target 8): biggest market, weakest public verification.**
- Source: **NPRA member directory filtered to CA** and **CPRS holders** first (the only third-party-verified signal in CA); then long-tenured independents surfacing via 5+ years of Google review history; consider established franchisee advisors (CarePatrol/Oasis/SCA) only if they pass the conflict questions.
- Metros: San Diego (2), LA/Orange County (2), Bay Area (2), Sacramento (1), Inland Empire (1).
- Verification burden is highest here — apply the full checklist including references.

**Phase 4 — Nevada (target 3).**
- Source: NPRA members in Las Vegas (2) and Reno (1); check **DPBH registration status** given the SB 260/2025 transition; require the NRS 449.114 disclosure document.

**Phase 5 — Idaho (target 2).**
- Source: Boise metro (2). Thinnest market, no regulation — lean hardest on CPRS/CSA, years in business via Idaho SOS, references, and review longevity. NPRA directory + local hospital discharge-planner recommendations are the practical channels.

**Outreach angle (all states):** "We're building a family-facing directory that only lists placement professionals who can document what the best in the industry already do — state registration, written fee disclosure, insurance, and local in-person service. Listing is free for the founding cohort; we verify rather than sell placement leads, so your listing signals what A Place for Mom can't." Lead with the WaPo/Senate scrutiny of national platforms as the reason families are looking for vetted locals — it is the industry's own talking point (OSRAA/ASRP/NPRA formed largely to professionalize against that reputation).

**Sequencing rationale:** OR→WA first builds a fully verified core and credibility with the associations (which are interconnected — OSRAA covers SW Washington too); CA is volume; NV/ID round out coverage. 6+6+8+3+2 = 25.
