Every facility you tour will show you a lobby, a menu, and a smiling salesperson. None of those predict whether your mother will be well cared for on a random Tuesday night in February. These ten qualities do — and every one of them is something you can check for yourself, on a tour or in public records.

## 1. Staff who have been there for years

Nothing predicts good care like caregivers who stay. Long-tenured aides know that your father gets dizzy when he stands up fast, and they notice the small changes that catch problems early. Industry surveys have put annual caregiver turnover near half of staff or more — so a building full of familiar faces is genuinely rare and genuinely valuable. See our [staffing ratios guide](/guides/staffing-ratios/) for the full picture.

**How to verify:** as you tour, ask front-line aides — not the marketing director — "How long have you worked here?" Three answers over three years each tells you more than any brochure.

## 2. Real staffing on nights and weekends

Tours happen on weekday mornings, when staffing peaks. Falls and medical crises don't keep those hours. A well-run facility staffs its worst shifts deliberately and can tell you exactly how, including whether overnight staff are awake and whether a nurse is in the building or only "on call."

**How to verify:** ask for caregiver numbers by shift — weekday day, weekend, overnight — for your parent's specific wing. Then drop by unannounced on a Saturday afternoon and count for yourself.

> **Ask this:** "How many hands-on caregivers are scheduled on this wing overnight and on weekends — and are the overnight staff awake?" A facility proud of its answer gives you numbers. A facility that talks around the question just answered it anyway.

## 3. Leadership you can see on the floor

In good buildings, the administrator knows residents by name, aides flag them down comfortably, and they've held the job for years. Administrator churn is one of the quietest predictors of decline — when leadership turns over, staff follow, and care wobbles for months.

**How to verify:** ask how long the administrator and head nurse have been in their roles. Watch how staff react when the administrator walks past: warm greetings are a great sign; sudden stiffness is not.

## 4. Residents doing something at 2 p.m.

Walk through mid-afternoon and look. In a well-run community, you'll find residents at activities, chatting, outside, or busy with staff — not rows of wheelchairs parked at a blaring television. Engagement isn't a luxury; boredom and isolation feed depression and decline. Our [activities and engagement guide](/guides/activities-and-engagement/) covers what a real program looks like.

**How to verify:** compare the posted activity calendar with reality. If the calendar says "music hour, 2:00" and the activity room is dark at 2:15, the calendar is marketing.

## 5. Food that people actually want to eat

Residents eat here three times a day, every day — food is quality of life, and poor food is often the first symptom of budget cuts. Good facilities serve meals that look and smell appealing, offer real alternatives, and get food to the table hot.

**How to verify:** eat a meal there, unannounced if possible, and watch the plates coming back — full plates mean the food isn't being eaten. Ask residents what they think; they will tell you. More in our [food and dining guide](/guides/food-and-dining/).

## 6. Clean, but still a home

You want clean floors and no odor of urine in the hallways — a persistent smell means slow response to incontinence care, full stop. But you don't want a sterile showroom either. Look for the good kind of mess: personal doors decorated, artwork at wheelchair height, a lived-in common room. People do better in places that feel like theirs.

**How to verify:** use your nose in the hallways farthest from the lobby, and peek at resident room doors — personalization means residents are treated as people, not occupants.

## 7. Straight answers about money

Well-run facilities hand you a complete fee schedule in writing: base rent, care-level charges, community fees, and what triggers a rate increase. Evasive pricing is rarely an accident — it usually hides steep care-level surcharges that appear after move-in.

**How to verify:** ask for the current fee schedule and the last three years of rate increases, in writing. A confident operator produces both without flinching.

## 8. They'll tell you no

This one surprises families: one of the best signs is a facility that declines admissions it can't serve well. Right-fit admissions mean they assess your parent honestly and say "we're not the right level of care" when it's true. A facility that takes everyone is filling beds, not matching care — and the resident pays for that later.

**How to verify:** ask, "What kinds of residents do you turn away, and what would make you ask someone to move out?" Specific, thoughtful answers are gold. "We can handle anything" is a sales line, not a care plan.

## 9. A clean inspection record — and honesty about the blemishes

Every licensed facility gets inspected, and nearly every facility eventually gets cited for something. What separates good operators is the pattern (isolated paperwork issues vs. repeat care failures) and the posture — good ones discuss citations openly and explain what they fixed. Learn to read the records with our guide to [inspection reports and ratings](/guides/reading-inspection-reports-and-ratings/).

**How to verify:** look up state inspection reports before the tour, then ask about anything you found. Watch whether they answer plainly or get defensive — the reaction tells you as much as the report.

## 10. Families hear news first, not last

In well-run facilities, communication is proactive: they call when your mother falls, when her medication changes, when she skips two meals — before you ask. In poorly run ones, families learn about problems weeks later, or from the hospital.

**How to verify:** ask exactly when and how families are notified about falls, medication changes, and condition changes. Then ask a visiting family member in the parking lot whether that's how it really works.

**What insiders know:** these ten qualities travel together, because they all flow from the same two sources — enough staff and stable leadership. A facility strong on tenure, night staffing, and honest pricing will almost always be strong on food and activities too. So if you're short on time, verify #1, #2, and #3 first; the rest usually follows. Bring our [touring checklist](/guides/touring-checklist/) to keep the visit organized.

## Common questions

**Do I really need all ten, or is seven out of ten good enough?**
No facility is perfect, and trade-offs are normal — a plain building with tenured staff beats a gorgeous one with a revolving door. But weight them unevenly: weakness in staffing, leadership, or honesty (#1, #2, #3, #7, #8) matters far more than an uninspired activity calendar.

**How can I check these if my parent needs a place quickly?**
Prioritize one unannounced visit at an off-peak time, the shift-level staffing question, and a look at state inspection reports online — those three can be done in a day. You can also place a parent somewhere adequate now and keep looking; moves are hard, but they are allowed.

**Do these qualities apply to small homes as well as big communities?**
Yes, though the form changes. In an [adult family home](/guides/adult-family-homes/), "leadership on the floor" means the owner personally provides care, and staffing depth means asking who covers when the owner sleeps or takes a day off. The underlying question — stable, sufficient, honest people — is identical.

**The facility scores well on everything but is expensive. Is it worth it?**
Often, yes — but verify that the price buys care, not chandeliers. A mid-priced building with tenured staff routinely outperforms a luxury one that skimps on aides. Price correlates with quality far less than families assume, which is exactly why this list exists.
