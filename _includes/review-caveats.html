Whatever star average you find on those sites is a starting point, not a verdict. We don't write these reviews, verify what they say, or investigate the community ourselves — that's exactly why we send you to the source instead of showing you a number. Before you weigh what you find, know what it can hide:

  • Who writes them. Usually adult children, not the resident — so reviews capture communication, billing, and first impressions more than the texture of daily care. More →
  • Emotion moves the score. People post when they're delighted or furious; the quietly satisfied middle rarely does, and reviews written in grief, guilt, or relief carry that charge. The calm three-star reviews usually hold the real trade-offs. More →
  • Reviews can be solicited or self-reported. A community can fairly ask happy families to post — or cross the line by gating out unhappy ones, pressuring residents, or paying. A burst of five-star reviews in a single week is a flag, not a triumph. More →
  • Counts and dates matter. A 5.0 from three reviews is a coin flip, and new management makes a 2019 review irrelevant. Small homes gather few reviews by nature — lean on the state record and your own visit. Small homes →
  • Government stars are a different thing. Medicare's nursing-home stars aren't reviews at all — they come from inspections, staffing, and clinical data. How those work →
