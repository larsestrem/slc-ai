We run the directory families use to research senior care, so we spend every day watching how they
actually search and what makes them call. Here's the honest version of what works, free and with no
email wall. The theme runs through all of it: the marketing that ranks and converts is the same
marketing that's truthful and legal. Trying to game search engines or manufacture a reputation
works for a while and then fails badly, sometimes with a regulator attached. Helpful, honest
representation is not just the ethical path; it's the one that actually performs.

## SEO: earn it, don't game it

Search engines are trying to reward the page that best answers a family's real question. So the
most durable SEO strategy is to *be* that page:

- **Answer the questions families actually ask.** Costs, care levels, what to expect, how to visit.
  The more genuinely useful your content is from the family's point of view, the better you rank and
  the more relevant the traffic you earn. That is the whole game.
- **Get local basics right.** A complete, accurate Google Business Profile, consistent name, address,
  and phone everywhere, and clear location pages. Most senior living searches are local.
- **Write for people, then format for machines.** Clear headings, honest titles, and structured
  data describing your community help search engines and the AI assistants families increasingly ask
  first. Formatting amplifies good content; it can't rescue thin content.
- **Never use tricks.** Hidden text, keyword stuffing, bought links, doorway pages, fake locations.
  Search engines catch these, and the penalty erases far more than the trick ever gained.

## Reviews: gather them the right way, and know the law

Reviews are one of the most powerful things in senior living marketing, and one of the easiest
places to break the law without realizing it. The line is bright:

- **Ask everyone, consistently.** Make it a simple, routine habit to invite honest reviews from
  families and residents. Volume of genuine reviews beats everything.
- **Never filter by sentiment ("review gating").** Asking only happy families for public reviews,
  or screening people to a private form first if they seem unhappy, violates the **FTC's rules** and
  most platforms' terms. Ask the same way regardless of how you expect someone to feel.
- **Never buy, fake, or incentivize reviews.** The FTC's 2024 rule bans fake and undisclosed
  incentivized reviews, with real financial penalties. Don't pay for reviews, don't write your own,
  don't trade anything for a rating.
- **Respond to all of them, especially the hard ones,** and do it without ever revealing a
  resident's private health details. Our page on
  [responding to reviews](/best-practices/responding-to-reviews/) covers exactly how, including the
  privacy line you must never cross.

Handled honestly, your review profile becomes your best marketing. Handled the wrong way, it becomes
a legal liability.

## Site speed and the basics that quietly cost you tours

Traffic only matters if the visitor stays and calls. The unglamorous fixes often move the needle
most:

- **Fast pages.** Slow load times lose visitors and hurt rankings. Compress images, cut bloat, and
  test on a phone, which is how most families first find you.
- **Mobile-first and accessible.** Readable type, tap-friendly buttons, and accessibility that also
  serves the adult children searching at 11 p.m. and the older prospect reading it themselves.
- **An obvious next step.** A clear phone number and an easy way to ask a question or book a tour on
  every page. Buried contact paths and pricing walls cost real inquiries.

## Content that serves the family (and quietly does your marketing)

The more you share that genuinely informs and helps a visitor from *their* perspective, the more you
earn their trust and the search ranking that follows:

- **Be specific and honest.** Real photos, real descriptions of daily life, plain answers about
  costs and care. Specifics build trust; superlatives ("luxury," "best," "award-winning") build
  suspicion and often can't be substantiated.
- **Show, don't claim.** A day in the life, your dining program, how you handle a concern. Let
  families conclude you're good rather than telling them.
- **Keep claims truthful and provable.** Under truth-in-advertising law, marketing claims must be
  accurate and substantiated. Don't promise outcomes or safety you can't back up.

## The legal lines you cannot cross

Keep these in view on everything you publish:

- **FTC review rules.** No fake, incentivized-without-disclosure, or sentiment-gated reviews.
- **Truth in advertising.** Every claim honest and substantiated; no guarantees you can't keep.
- **Resident privacy (HIPAA).** Never use a resident's story, photo, or health details without their
  clear written consent, and never disclose health information publicly, including to rebut a review.
- **Fair housing and accessibility.** Marketing and imagery that don't discriminate, and a website
  usable by people with disabilities.

None of this is legal advice; when a specific question comes up, ask a qualified attorney. But the
short version is simple, and it's the same message as the rest of this site: represent yourself
honestly, serve the family first, and the marketing takes care of itself.
