You did the research, made the decision, and moved your parent in — and it turns out the job isn't over; it changed. In any senior living community, the residents who do best are the ones whose families stay engaged, visible, and constructively firm. This page is your manual for that new role: what's normal, how to raise concerns so they get fixed, and who backs you up when they don't.

## Weeks 1-6: what's normal and what isn't

Almost every new resident goes through an adjustment period, and it's often rough. In the first several weeks, it's **normal** to see: sadness, anger, "take me home" phone calls, eating less, sleeping oddly, skipping activities, and idealizing the old house. This is grief, and it usually softens by weeks four to six as routines and faces become familiar. Guilt will tell you the sadness means you chose wrong; usually it means change is hard. Our guide on [coping with the decision](/guides/coping-with-the-decision/) is written for exactly this stretch.

**Worry — and act — regardless of the calendar** if you see: rapid weight loss, dehydration, unexplained bruises or skin breakdown, sudden confusion (in an older adult, often a urinary tract infection or a medication problem, not "just decline"), overly sedated behavior, missed medications, or a parent who seems afraid of a particular staff member. These are clinical red flags, not adjustment. Raise them the same day.

What insiders know: visit at unpredictable times, including evenings and weekends, when staffing is thinnest. A community looks its best Tuesday at 10 a.m. It *is* its best — or isn't — Saturday at 7 p.m.

## Care conferences: your standing tool

Licensed communities hold periodic **care conferences** (also called care plan meetings — the [glossary](/guides/glossary/) decodes this and the rest of the vocabulary) — [nursing homes](/guides/skilled-nursing/) must hold them on a regular schedule, and [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/) communities hold service-plan reviews under state rules. Two things families don't know:

- **You can request one at any time.** You don't have to wait for the scheduled review. Any significant change — a fall, weight loss, new confusion, a concern that isn't getting fixed — justifies a conference.
- **You're entitled to be there** (with your parent's consent, or as their representative), including by phone or video if you live far away.

Who attends: typically the nurse or care coordinator, an aide who knows your parent, dietary and activities staff, and — in nursing homes — social services. Bring: a short written list of your top three concerns, your own notes with dates, any medication questions, and one positive observation to open with. Leave with: specific commitments, who owns each one, and a date to check back. Then send a short thank-you email summarizing what was agreed — you've just created a friendly paper trail.

> **Ask this:** at any care conference — "What changes have you noticed in my mother in the last month, and has her care plan changed to match?" The answer tells you whether the plan is a living document or a form in a drawer.

## The escalation ladder

When something's wrong, most problems are fixed at the lowest level — and going straight to the top for a cold breakfast burns goodwill you'll want later. Climb in order, but know when to jump:

1. **The aide or caregiver** — for day-to-day, in-the-moment issues. Kindly and directly.
2. **The charge nurse or care coordinator** — for anything clinical, recurring, or involving medications.
3. **The executive director / administrator** — when a problem persists after two tries below, or involves billing, staffing patterns, or policy. Put it in writing (email), ask for a response date.
4. **Corporate or the owner** — if the building's leadership is the problem. Chain communities have regional directors; the ombudsman (below) can tell you who to write.
5. **A state complaint** — every state licenses these communities and investigates complaints; you can file even while working other channels. Our guide to [checking complaints and state records](/guides/checking-complaints-and-state-records/) shows where, and [reading inspection reports and ratings](/guides/reading-inspection-reports-and-ratings/) shows how your complaint fits the record.
6. **The Long-Term Care Ombudsman** — not truly the last rung; you can call them at *any* step for advice, and they'll advocate without naming you if you fear retaliation.

**Skip steps immediately** for anything involving safety, suspected abuse or neglect, theft, or a medical emergency: go straight to the administrator and the state (and, for suspected abuse, Adult Protective Services or the police). Serious harm is never a "start with the aide" conversation.

## Document as you go

Keep a simple log — a notes app is fine. For every concern: date, what you observed, who you told, what they said, and what changed. Photograph anything visible (bruises, injuries, an unclean room) with your phone; the timestamp travels with the photo. This isn't lawsuit preparation — it's memory. "Third missed shower this month, here are the dates" gets action; "she never gets showers" gets a shrug and a defensive reply. If you ever do need the state or ombudsman, your log turns a vague grievance into an investigable complaint.

## Firm and welcome are not opposites

Here's the uncomfortable truth: staff are human, and residents whose families are *present* get more attentive care. Not because anyone decides to neglect the others — but visits create accountability, and familiar families are easier to communicate with. The families who get the best results run on three tracks at once:

- **Present.** Regular visits at varied times; staff know your name and know you'll be back.
- **Appreciative.** Learn the aides' names. Thank people specifically ("Maria always gets Dad laughing"). Praise good staff to the administrator — it's rarer than complaints and gets remembered.
- **Clear.** When something's wrong, say so plainly, in writing when it matters, without apology or fury. Warmth is not a substitute for standards, and standards don't require hostility.

The family that's only ever appreciative gets taken for granted. The family that's only ever complaining gets managed. Be both, and you get partnership.

> **Ask this:** to the executive director, a month in — "Who should I talk to first when I have a concern, and how do you prefer I escalate if it isn't resolved?" You're mapping the ladder before you need it — and telling them, pleasantly, that you'll use it.

## Gifts and gratitude, done right

Most communities prohibit staff from accepting cash or individual gifts — don't put a beloved aide's job at risk by pressing an envelope on her. What works instead: food for the whole shift, a group holiday gift through the front office, thank-you notes naming names, and a letter to the administrator praising individuals — that one goes in personnel files and matters at raise time.

## When the care level no longer fits

Part of your job is watching for the mismatch between your parent's needs and the building's abilities. Warning signs: repeated falls, staff calling you to "come help," long stretches left in bed or in front of the TV, medication errors, wandering in a non-secure building, or hints about "a higher level of care." Don't wait for a forced move — request a care conference, ask directly whether they can still meet your parent's needs (and at what cost), and research the next level while you have time. A planned move beats a 30-day notice every time.

## Common questions

**Will complaining make staff treat my parent worse?**
Retaliation is illegal, and with reasonable, documented families it's rare — visible, engaged families typically see *better* care, not worse. If you genuinely fear it, that fear is itself important information; call the ombudsman, who can act without identifying you.

**How often should I visit?**
Rhythm beats volume: a predictable weekly visit at varying hours does more than a monthly marathon. If you're far away, schedule video calls, join care conferences by phone, and recruit a local friend or a paid geriatric care manager as eyes on the ground.

**What exactly does the ombudsman do?**
Every state runs a Long-Term Care Ombudsman program under federal law: trained advocates who work only for residents — never for facilities — in nursing homes and, in most states, assisted living. They visit communities, resolve complaints, explain your rights, and it's free and confidential. Their contact info must be posted in the building.

**My parent complains constantly, but the staff seem fine. Who do I believe?**
Verify, don't dismiss. Show up unannounced at the times the complaints describe, check the specifics (Is the food cold? Is call-light response slow?), and treat patterns — same complaint, same details, different days — as signal.

## Where to get help

- **Long-Term Care Ombudsman** — the resident's free, confidential advocate; find yours at ltcombudsman.org or posted in the facility.
- **Your state licensing agency** — accepts and investigates complaints about care and conditions; filing is free and can be anonymous in most states.
- **Adult Protective Services** — for suspected abuse, neglect, or exploitation; in an emergency, call 911 first.
- **Eldercare Locator: 1-800-677-1116** — connects you to your Area Agency on Aging, local family-caregiver support programs, and the ombudsman.
