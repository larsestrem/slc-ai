Every licensed senior care facility has a public paper trail: inspections, complaints, and —
this is the part most families never see — the *resolutions*. Reading that trail is one of the
most revealing things you can do before choosing a community. But it only helps if you read it
fairly. This page shows you where to look in each state we cover, and how to tell the
difference between a facility with a problem and a facility that solves problems.

## Where to look, state by state

| State | Where to check | What you'll find |
|---|---|---|
| Oregon | [Oregon LTC Licensing search](https://ltclicensing.oregon.gov/) | Licenses, inspection history, substantiated complaints and abuse findings — one of the most transparent systems in the country |
| California | [CDSS Care Facility Search](https://www.ccld.dss.ca.gov/carefacilitysearch/) (assisted living/RCFE) and [Cal Health Find](https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/CHCQ/LCP/CalHealthFind/) (nursing homes) | Inspection reports, complaint counts and outcomes, citations |
| Washington | [DSHS facility lookup](https://fortress.wa.gov/dshs/adsaapps/lookup/) | Licenses, inspection and enforcement history for [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/) and [adult family homes](/guides/adult-family-homes/) |
| Arizona | [AZ Care Check](https://azcarecheck.azdhs.gov/) | Inspections and enforcement actions for licensed care facilities |
| Nevada | [Bureau of Health Care Quality & Compliance](https://dpbh.nv.gov/Reg/HealthFacilities/HealthFacilities_Home/) | Licensing and survey information |
| Idaho | [Bureau of Facility Standards](https://healthandwelfare.idaho.gov/) | Survey and complaint investigation letters (request if not posted) |
| Utah | [Utah DHHS facility licensing](https://dhhs.utah.gov/licensing/) | License status and findings |
| Colorado | [CDPHE health facility search](https://cdphe.colorado.gov/health-facilities) | Licenses, occurrences, and survey findings |
| Hawaii | [Office of Health Care Assurance](https://health.hawaii.gov/ohca/) | Inspection reports for licensed facilities |
| Any state — nursing homes | [Medicare Care Compare](https://www.medicare.gov/care-compare/) | Federal inspections, staffing data, penalties, and star ratings for every certified SNF |

Two more sources worth a call anywhere: your **Long-Term Care Ombudsman** (resident advocates
who know each building's complaint patterns) and your **Area Agency on Aging**.

## Read the record fairly — this part matters

Here's what those records look like from the inside. Caring for dozens of seniors is one of
the hardest jobs there is. Staffing is the industry's permanent struggle — hiring is hard,
retention is harder, and the work is physically and emotionally demanding. And needs don't
arrive politely one at a time: two residents can fall during the same dinner service a third
is having a medical emergency. Sometimes several things go wrong in parallel, beyond what any
reasonable staffing plan anticipated. Hospitals get complaints. Schools get complaints. Every
facility that operates long enough will have something in its file.

**So a complaint — even a substantiated one, even a corrective action — is not automatically
disqualifying. A problem that was fixed is no longer a problem.** Regulators issuing a citation
and the facility correcting it is the system *working*, not failing.

What you're actually looking for is the difference between incidents and patterns:

- **A good sign, believe it or not:** an occasional citation with a prompt, specific plan of
  correction and no repeat. That's a facility that takes the process seriously.
- **Worth a conversation:** a recent citation — ask the administrator about it directly on
  your tour. A confident, specific answer about what changed tells you more than a spotless
  record does.
- **A real warning:** the *same* deficiency appearing inspection after inspection, complaints
  that cluster around one theme (medication errors, unexplained injuries, retaliation),
  findings the facility contested rather than corrected, or leadership that gets defensive
  when you ask. Indifference is the disqualifier — not the incident.

> **Ask this:** "Tell me about your most recent state citation or substantiated complaint —
> what happened, and what did you change?" A well-run facility answers this without
> flinching. Evasion is the answer, too.

## Context: some facility types score lower by nature

Keep the playing field level in your head. [Skilled nursing facilities](/guides/skilled-nursing/)
carry more complaints and citations than assisted living almost everywhere — not necessarily
because they're worse, but because they care for the sickest residents, are inspected far more
intensively under federal rules, and are often reviewed online by families going through the
most stressful weeks of their lives. A nursing home with a handful of corrected citations may
be performing better, relative to the difficulty of its job, than an assisted living community
with a clean file. That's why we judge SNFs primarily on their
[CMS star ratings](/guides/reading-inspection-reports-and-ratings/) rather than consumer
reviews — and why you should read every record against the type of care being delivered.

The goal isn't to find a facility with a spotless past. It's to find one that's honest about
its hard days and visibly putting in the effort to reconcile anything that isn't going right.
Those are the communities worth your shortlist.

## Common questions

**The facility has a substantiated complaint. Should I walk away?**
Not on that alone. Read what happened, how long ago, and what the correction was — then ask
the administrator about it. Walk away when you see repetition, a cluster around the same
issue, or defensiveness.

**What's the difference between a complaint and a citation?**
A complaint is an allegation someone filed; the state investigates and marks it substantiated
or unsubstantiated. A citation (or deficiency) is the state's own finding that a rule wasn't
met, usually from an inspection — it comes with a required plan of correction.

**Are online reviews or state records more reliable?**
They answer different questions. State records are investigated facts about care and safety;
reviews are impressions of daily life — food, friendliness, communication. Use both, and
weigh [reviews with their context](/guides/reading-inspection-reports-and-ratings/).

**The state website is confusing. Is there a shortcut?**
Call your Long-Term Care Ombudsman (find yours via the Eldercare Locator, 1-800-677-1116).
They can tell you a facility's complaint history — and its reputation among residents — in
one conversation.
