If you're reading this on your phone in a hospital hallway, take a breath. Someone just told you your parent can't go home, handed you a list of facilities, and mentioned a deadline. This page walks you through the next 48 hours, step by step - including rights you have that nobody in that hallway is required to volunteer.

## Step 1: Understand whose clock you're on

The person managing the exit is a **discharge planner** (or "case manager"). They are usually kind, often overworked, and they work for the hospital. Hospitals are paid in ways that reward moving patients out quickly - once your parent is medically stable, every extra day costs the hospital money.

That doesn't make the planner your enemy. It means their timeline is the hospital's timeline, not your family's. Their list of rehab facilities is typically **whoever has an open bed**, not a ranked quality list - federal rules generally forbid them from steering you, so the list arrives with no opinions attached. What insiders know: the pressure to "pick by tomorrow" is a business deadline, not always a medical one. You are allowed to push back politely, and this page shows you how.

## Step 2: Know your appeal right - care continues while you use it

If your parent is on Medicare and you believe the discharge is too soon or unsafe, you have a formal fast-appeal right. Here's how it works:

- The hospital must give you a written notice of discharge (the "Important Message from Medicare") with a phone number for your state's **Quality Improvement Organization (QIO)** - an independent medical review group, not part of the hospital.
- Call the QIO **before midnight on the planned discharge day**. Say you are appealing the discharge.
- The QIO reviews the medical record, usually within about a day, and decides whether discharge is appropriate.
- **Your parent stays in the hospital during the review, and you generally aren't charged for those days while the appeal is pending.**

Even when families lose the appeal, it often buys 24-48 more hours - enough time to choose well instead of fast. Medicare Advantage plans have an equivalent fast-appeal process. Ask the discharge planner for the notice and the QIO number; they're required to provide it.

> **Ask this:** "I'd like the Important Message from Medicare and the QIO's phone number, please." You don't have to justify it or argue. Asking calmly signals you know your rights - and planners slow down for families who do.

## Step 3: Check the status that decides who pays

Before anything else, ask one question: **"Is my parent admitted as an inpatient, or under observation status?"**

Traditional Medicare generally covers a rehab stay in a skilled nursing facility only after **three consecutive inpatient midnights** in the hospital. Nights spent "under observation" - even in a regular hospital bed, even for days - may not count. Families discover this after discharge, when the rehab bill arrives uncovered. It's one of the most expensive traps in American healthcare.

If your parent is on observation status and rehab is being discussed, ask the doctor whether inpatient admission is medically justified, and ask what Medicare will actually cover. Some Medicare Advantage plans waive the three-day rule but require prior authorization instead - call the plan's number on the insurance card. Our [skilled nursing guide](/guides/skilled-nursing/) explains the full coverage picture, and [Medicaid vs. Medicare](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/) covers what happens if a short stay turns long.

## Step 4: Know what Medicare rehab coverage really means

When people say "Medicare covers 100 days of rehab," here's the fine print, briefly:

- Days 1-20 are covered in full (traditional Medicare, after a qualifying stay).
- Days 21-100 carry a daily coinsurance of roughly $200+ - a good Medigap policy usually picks this up.
- Coverage lasts only while your parent needs **daily skilled care** - most rehab stays run 20-40 days, not 100.
- If the facility says coverage is ending and you disagree, that decision can be appealed too. "She's plateaued" is not a valid reason to cut coverage under Medicare policy (per the Jimmo v. Sebelius settlement) - your SHIP counselor can help you appeal.

## Step 5: Vet the list in one evening

You can meaningfully screen rehab facilities in a few hours, from a phone. For each name on the list:

1. **Look it up on Medicare.gov Care Compare.** Note the overall star rating, but click into the **health inspection** rating (the most trustworthy piece) and the **staffing** tab - daily staffing predicts your parent's actual experience better than anything else. Screen out 1- and 2-star health inspections. Our guide to [reading inspection reports and ratings](/guides/reading-inspection-reports-and-ratings/) shows what the numbers mean.
2. **Skim recent Google reviews** - read the 2- and 3-star ones from the last year, which tend to be the most honest, and remember rehab reviews skew harsh everywhere.
3. **Make one phone call** to your top pick: "How many minutes of therapy per day, how many days a week? What's your nurse and aide staffing on nights and weekends?" A confident, specific answer is a good sign.
4. **Drive past or send a scout if you can.** Ten minutes in the lobby mid-morning - how it smells, whether residents are up and dressed, how staff speak to them - tells you things no website will. Bring our [touring checklist](/guides/touring-checklist/) if you get inside.

## Step 6: Remember - this is not a permanent placement

Here is the sentence that lowers everyone's blood pressure: **choosing a rehab facility is not choosing where your parent will live.** A rehab stay is temporary. If the facility disappoints, you can transfer. When rehab ends, your parent can go home, move to [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/), or move to a different facility entirely - and families often should, because the best rehab program and the best long-term home are rarely the same building.

The one caveat: if there's a real chance rehab becomes a long-term stay, pick a facility you could accept longer-term, and ask whether it accepts Medicaid. Otherwise, make a good-enough choice for a few weeks and save the big decision for when you can breathe.

> **Ask this:** to the discharge planner - "Which facilities on this list have a Medicare health-inspection rating of 3 stars or better?" They may not be allowed to recommend, but they can answer factual questions - and the question tells you which names to check first.

## Step 7: If no good option has a bed

Sometimes every decent facility is full and the only open beds are at places with alarming ratings. You still have moves:

- **Say the words "unsafe discharge."** Tell the planner, calmly, that you don't believe a safe discharge plan exists yet and you'd like alternatives - and file the QIO appeal to hold the timeline.
- **Ask about home health instead.** For some patients, going home with Medicare-covered home health therapy and nursing visits is a legitimate alternative to a poor facility. Ask the doctor directly if it's an option.
- **Widen the map.** A great facility 40 minutes away beats a troubled one 5 minutes away - for a few weeks, the drive is survivable.
- **Take a bed as a bridge.** If you must accept a mediocre facility, keep checking daily for an opening at your first choice and transfer. Facilities admit transfers from other facilities all the time.

## Common questions

**Can the hospital discharge my parent if I refuse to pick a facility?**
The hospital can't physically force a transfer, but if your parent is deemed medically ready and you've exhausted appeals, the hospital may start charging privately for extra days. The QIO appeal is your leverage - use it early rather than digging in without it.

**Does my parent have to go to a facility on the hospital's list?**
No. The list is just facilities with open beds that the hospital contacted. You can ask about any Medicare-certified facility; the planner can check bed availability there for you.

**What if my parent refuses rehab entirely?**
A mentally competent adult can refuse and go home, even against advice. Ask the doctor to order home health services, be honest with your parent about the risks, and know that a failed week at home often makes them more open to rehab - though the Medicare qualifying-stay clock may reset.

**Is the facility the hospital "recommends" a safe bet?**
Planners generally can't recommend, so any strong steer deserves gentle skepticism - some hospitals own or partner with rehab facilities. Check the stars yourself; it takes ten minutes.

## Where to get help

- **Medicare.gov Care Compare** - ratings, staffing, and inspection results for every certified facility.
- **Your state's QIO** - the fast-appeal line; the number is on the Important Message from Medicare, or call 1-800-MEDICARE.
- **State Health Insurance Assistance Program (SHIP)** - free counselors who can walk you through appeal rights, observation status, and coverage questions.
- **Eldercare Locator: 1-800-677-1116** - connects you to your Area Agency on Aging for local options, including home-based care.
