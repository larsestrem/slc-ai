If your parent's savings won't stretch to cover care in Oregon, you're not out of options - and you're not alone. Oregon Medicaid does pay for long-term care, and Oregon has ranked among the stronger states in national long-term-care scorecards for offering that help outside a nursing home. This page explains what's covered, who qualifies, and how to start.

## What Oregon's Medicaid program is called

Oregon's Medicaid program is the **Oregon Health Plan (OHP)**. Long-term care services for seniors are managed by a division of the Oregon Department of Human Services called **Aging and People with Disabilities (APD)**. When you apply for help paying for a nursing home, [assisted living](/guides/assisted-living/), or an [adult foster home](/guides/adult-family-homes/), you'll be working with APD - not the regular OHP application most people use for health insurance.

That distinction matters. Qualifying for regular OHP coverage does not automatically qualify someone for long-term care help. Long-term care Medicaid has its own financial rules and requires a separate care-needs assessment.

## Yes, it pays for nursing homes

Like every state, Oregon Medicaid pays for nursing home ([skilled nursing](/guides/skilled-nursing/) facility) care for people who qualify financially and medically. If your parent is in a Medicaid-certified nursing home and meets the rules, Medicaid covers the cost of care, room, and board. Most of their monthly income then goes toward the cost of care, with a small personal needs allowance kept back.

Don't confuse this with Medicare, which only pays for short rehab stays after a hospitalization - our [Medicaid vs. Medicare guide](/guides/medicaid-vs-medicare/) walks through the difference.

## Oregon is unusually good at paying for care outside nursing homes

Here's something many families don't know: Oregon has spent decades building alternatives to nursing homes, and its Medicaid program reflects that. Through the **K Plan** (Oregon's name for the federal Community First Choice option) and APD's home and community-based services, Oregon Medicaid pays for care in:

- **Adult foster homes** - small licensed homes with five or fewer residents, a signature Oregon option
- **Assisted living facilities** and **residential care facilities**
- **[Memory care](/guides/memory-care/) communities**
- Your parent's own home or a family member's home
- Adult day services

The key limitation: in assisted living, adult foster homes, and memory care, Medicaid pays for the **care services** - help with bathing, dressing, medications, supervision - but not the full **room and board** cost. Room and board is typically paid from your parent's own income (often most of their Social Security check), at a state-set rate that is far lower than private-pay pricing. The exact arrangement varies by setting, so confirm the numbers with the APD case manager.

One honest caveat: not every facility accepts Medicaid, and some accept it only after a resident has private-paid for a year or more. Always ask a facility directly whether they take Medicaid from day one, and what happens if money runs out later.

> **Ask this:** "Do you accept Oregon Medicaid (APD/K Plan) residents, and if my mom spends down her savings here, can she stay on Medicaid in the same room?"

## Who qualifies: the basic shape

Eligibility has two parts, and both must be met:

- **Care need:** an APD assessor determines whether your parent needs a nursing-facility level of care - meaningful help with daily activities like bathing, dressing, mobility, or supervision for dementia.
- **Finances:** there are income and asset limits. For a single applicant, countable assets generally must be quite low (around $2,000 in recent years), with a monthly income cap. A healthy spouse remaining at home is allowed to keep a much larger share of the couple's assets and income under federal spousal protection rules.

Oregon, like nearly all states, applies a **five-year look-back**: gifts or below-market transfers made in the five years before applying can trigger a penalty period. Don't move money around before talking to a professional. Exact dollar limits change every year - confirm current figures with APD or an elder law attorney rather than relying on numbers you find online.

## Waitlists and capacity

Because the K Plan is a state-plan benefit rather than a capped waiver, Oregon generally serves eligible people without the long waiver waitlists seen in some states. The practical bottleneck is usually finding a facility or adult foster home with an open Medicaid bed, especially in smaller towns. Expect to tour more places and be flexible on location. Confirm current availability with your local APD office.

## How to apply

- Call the **Aging and Disability Resource Connection (ADRC) of Oregon** at 1-855-673-2372. This is the front door - they'll explain options and route you to the right office.
- Contact your **local APD or Area Agency on Aging office** to start the financial application and request a care assessment.
- Gather documents first: bank statements (several years' worth), income sources, insurance policies, property records, and any trust documents.

The financial review is detailed and can take weeks. Start before the money runs out - ideally when your parent has six months of funds left, not six days.

## One more thing: estate recovery

After a Medicaid recipient dies, Oregon (like all states) is required to seek repayment from the estate for long-term care costs it paid. This is called estate recovery, and it can affect the family home. It's a real consideration, not a reason to avoid Medicaid - but families should understand it going in. Read our [Medicaid estate recovery guide](/guides/medicaid-estate-recovery/) and talk to an elder law attorney before making decisions about the house.

## Common questions

**Does Oregon Medicaid pay for assisted living?**
Yes - Oregon Medicaid pays for care services in licensed assisted living and residential care facilities for eligible residents, through APD's programs. Your parent's income still goes toward room and board, and not all facilities participate.

**What's an adult foster home, and does Medicaid cover it?**
Adult foster homes are small licensed care homes (five or fewer residents) common across Oregon, and yes, Medicaid pays for care in them for eligible residents. For many families they're a more affordable, homier alternative to a large facility.

**Can my dad get Medicaid if he owns a house?**
Often yes - a home is generally not counted while a spouse lives there, and may be exempt in other situations, but estate recovery can apply later. This is exactly the situation to review with an elder law attorney before applying.

**How long does approval take?**
Plan on 45 days or more once a complete application is in, plus time for the care assessment. Incomplete paperwork is the most common cause of delay.

## Where to get help

- **ADRC of Oregon:** 1-855-673-2372 - free help navigating long-term care options
- **Local APD offices** (Oregon Department of Human Services) - applications and assessments
- **Area Agencies on Aging** - local counseling and caregiver support
- **Oregon SHIBA** (Senior Health Insurance Benefits Assistance) - free Medicare/insurance counseling
- **Eldercare Locator:** 1-800-677-1116
- An **elder law attorney** for spend-down, spousal protection, and estate recovery questions

Rules and dollar limits change - always verify current details with the state before acting.
